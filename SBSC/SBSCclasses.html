SBSC 200 Introduction to Social and Behavioral Sciences
4 credits
This course is an introduction to the basic principles (theory and method) shared in common by the traditional sciences of anthropology, archaeology, sociology, psychology, political science, and allied fields of inquiry. Formerly known as SBSC 100A and 100B.

SBSC 201A Elementary Statistics:  Descriptive Statistics
201B Probability Distributions and Inference
201C Hypothesis Testing
4 credits
This course provides an overview as descriptive and inferential statistics used in the social and behavioral sciences and includes parametric and non-parametric approaches such as regression, correlation, hypothesis testing, and chi-square. Calculations are conducted using SPSS computer software. It is recommended that students have some computer skills.

SBSC 210A Archaeological Technologies
2 credits
This lab section provides a hands-on introduction to the basic technologies and tools of the laboratory and field archaeologist. Students will be introduced to the use of a range of technical equipment employed in archaeological survey, artifact analysis, and mapping projects. Though equipment and instruction will vary from year to year, specific technology types covered in this section include the pocket transit, theodolite, GPS (Global Positioning System), and computer-networked lab resources, such as digital calipers, electronic scales, and on-line database management tools. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 310A. Formerly known as a project-based learning lab.
Prerequisite(s): required of all students with major area concentrations in archaeology and museum studies

SBSC 210B Technical Writing: Lab And Fieldnote Preparation
2 credits
This computer-based lab section provides a basic introduction to technical or scientific writing for archaeologists and other social scientists. Though course content will vary, course objectives center on imparting those basic skills necessary for producing accurate technical descriptions, scientific reports, and clear and concise lab and fieldnote documents. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 310B.
Prerequisite(s): required of all students with major concentration areas in archaeology and museum studies

SBSC 210C Technical Imaging: Artifact and Historic Photo Restoration
2 credits
This lab practicum provides a hands-on introduction to those basic camera skills and photographic methods necessary for documenting archaeological sites and historic materials. Though course content will vary, the objective of this section is to provide archaeology and other social science students with the necessary skills for producing effective images in both film media and digital formats. Though equipment and instruction will vary from year to year, specific imaging technologies and methods covered in this section include studio or museum lighting, copywork, and computer-based digital scanning and image manipulation for the purposes of historic photo restoration and artifact reconstruction. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 310C.
Prerequisite(s): Optional for students with major area concentrations in archaeology and museum studies

SBSC 212 A Social and Political History of the United States
4 credits
A social and political history of the United States from gendered and multicultural perspectives covering the evolution of major political and social institutions. Attention is given to regional differences, immigration and ethnic formation, and the factors of race, sex, and gender in the social and political history of the United States. Formerly known as Survey Research Methods. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 313 and obtaining instructor consent

SBSC 222SL Visual Anthropology and Photography for the Social Sciences
2 credits
This course provides a basic introduction to the uses and applications of photography and film media in the social and behavioral sciences. Students will explore the potentials of photography and related applications of the study of visual anthropology and sociology, archaeological and documentary photography, and forensic and investigative methods and applications. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 322SL.
Prerequisite(s): A basic 35mm camera with a manual F-stop ring and speed indicator is required for the course.

SBSC 224SL Archaeology: From Map to Museum
4 credits
This course is an introduction to the methods, principles, and practices of field archaeology. It will combine in-class lab and discussion sessions with field studies in historical archaeology. Students will explore the methods and practices of archaeology and archaeologists by way of lab and field exercises that stress strategy, interpretation, description, information management, archaeological technologies, and the role of scientific inquiry in the human sciences. Students will first explore archaeological principles, methods and interpretations by way of introductory lab exercises ranging from the hands-on manufacture and use of stone tools through to the interpretation of ancient and modern material cultures. The second five weeks of this learning experience will entail on-site field excavations and analysis of Hispanic colonial artifacts at the Old Mission of San Juan Bautista. Students will be taught information management skills and strategies specific to organizing and interpreting diverse sources and types of information recovered as a result of the field excavation. Finally, the last third of the semester will be devoted to lab projects centered on the analysis and interpretation of that materials recovered in the field or developed in the lab. The course is designed to appeal to archaeology, museum studies or art history majors and liberal studies or teacher education students. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 324SL.

SBSC 226 History of the Monterey Bay: 10,000 B.C. to Steinbeck
4 credits
This course examines the natural, cultural and historical exploration of the resources, peoples, and cultures of Monterey Bay from the earliest non-Western indigenous developments through the 1930ís narratives of John Steinbeck. It offers a comparative survey of culture and ethnicity ranging from the original Ohlone and Esselen inhabitants of the Monterey Bay through to the successive landfalls of the Spanish, Mexican, Chinese, Anglo, Italian, and Japanese immigrants to these shores; and, as such, provides the broadest possible coverage of this regionís cultural and natural history. Student projects and learning experiences will include contributions to the development of an internet Web page devoted to the natural and cultural history of Monterey Bay, and scheduled field tours will provide opportunities for exploring the archaeological and historical sites of the central coast. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 326.

SBSC 248 Ancient Maya Civilization
4 credits
An exploration of the spectacular archaeological, epigraphic, and historical discoveries that have revolutionized emerging perspectives on the culture and civilization of the ancient Maya of southern Mexico and Central America. Classic Maya civilization, which has traditionally been dated to between A.D. 250 and 900, has been found to have evolved far earlier, and persisted far longer than once thought. Epigraphic or Mayan glyphic studies and research currently underway in the Maya heartland clearly attest to the antiquity and richness of a culture and civilization that evolved and endured in the Mesoamerican tropics for nearly three thousand years. Course content will draw upon an ever expanding world of ìvirtualî software-based glyphic translation and astronomical and calendrical correlation programs created for the purpose of probing directly into the rich literary, artistic, and historical traditions of recently recovered ancient Maya texts and monuments. This learning experience will examine the social, cultural, economic, political, and religious underpinnings of the Maya dynastic tradition in comparative and cross-cultural terms and from the perspective of landmark archaeological projects and glyphic interpretations, and their respective significance in deciphering the larger Maya lifeway. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 348.
Prerequisites: none.

SBSC 252 Human Landscape: Culture, Society, and Space
4 credits
This learning experience applies a geographical perspective to the study of environment, culture, human societies and their interactions. It examines regions around the world with a thematic approach, via such topics as population, language, religion, economic development, social customs, urbanization, and resource problems. Key spatial concepts, principles, and contemporary issues are the focus of this course. MLO addressed: AREA I (Theory). Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 352.

SBSC 253 Gender Relations and Development in Third-World Countries
4 credits
This course provides an overview of theories on gender and development, global gender division of labor, and relations of power within the contexts of third-world countries. It examines challenges and dilemmas in development theory and practices, as well as shortcomings in national and international statistics in accounting for womenís contributions to both formal and informal economy. It explores gender relations in all aspects of life such as work, family, decision making, health and child bearing, education, and social and political participation. It also examines the linkages between global and local uses of labor. This learning experience will provide a theory deliverable in gender and economic development partially fulfilling the SBSC MLO (Area I) in Theory. Students will also develop a deliverable partially fulfilling Area III, Regional Studies as defined in their approved ILP. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 353.

SBSC 258SL Community Politics
4 credits
This course will examine community politics and the role that institutions and society play in understanding the significance of the assets and needs of the community. It will include an examination of race, gender, and class dynamics and their relationship to multi-ethnic politics and the competition for scarce resources. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 358SL. Formerly known as SBSC 233SL.

SBSC 259SL Global Dimensions of Political Economy: Asian Linkages with American Lives
4 credits
This course provides an overview of theories on global restructuring processes and explores their political, economic, social, historical and cultural factors that impact the lives of Americans and Asian Pacific Rim peoples. Through Service-Learning class projects, it connects with local communities by focusing on contemporary issues such as farm workers, factory workers in the comeback of "sweat shops" in big US cities, maquiladoras industries or export processing zones. It examines theories on power relations between different cultures and gender. It provides concrete case studies on global industries such as agribusiness, textile and garment, electronics, and automobile. Major Learning Outcomes addressed by this learning opportunity include a theory deliverable in Political Economy (Area I) and a deliverable addressing the Disciplinary and Region Specific Studies (Area III). Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 359SL.

SBSC 262A Cultural Anthropology: Ethnographic Research Methods, Data Management and Analysis
4 credits
This course introduces students to the fundamental data collection, data management, and analysis methods used in ethnographic research. The course covers such areas as observation, participant observation, the use of interview guides, and the keeping of field journals. In addition, students learn such related how to develop the skills for gaining acceptance from study subjects (informants) and the recording and use of computerized qualitative data, including software for data ordering and management. Students will carry out actual field assignments and a field project. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 362A.

SBSC 262B Anthropology of Face-To-Face Communities: Studying Local Communities
4 credits
Examines the organization and dynamics of community life in face-to-face local communities. Special attention is given to community study methods and the uses of ethnographic data collection in communities. The course analyzes the primary units of a community, including families, social networks, households, social and ethnic/racial groups, associations (male, female, and mixed gender), and local forms of political involvement, empowerment, self help, and governance. Students carry out an ethnographic field study of an aspect of local community life. 4-6 units This learning experience addresses Area II (Research Methods) and Area III - Area Studies/Discipline Specific studies for the SBSC. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 362B.

SBSC 263A Community Ethnography
4 credits
This project based learning lab focuses on a variety of topics in the Social and Behavioral Sciences. Students taking this class will learn the basic skills required to conduct qualitative research. Students learn in this lab the application of basic ethnographic methods and strategies such as participant observation, field notes, interviewing, genealogies, and mapping. Based on preliminary data produced as they practice their research skills, students will identify and learn to write the different sections required to produce a fundable research proposal. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 363A. Formerly known as SBSC 310A.

SBSC 263B Learning Ethnographic Research in Multimedia Electronic Environment
4 credits
This course is designed to introduce students in social sciences to data collection strategies and methodologies for carrying out ethnographic field research, and recording, managing, and analyzing the information obtained. The curriculum and field research site is contained in a series of electronically simulated field exercises contained in a multimedia interactive learning package called an Ethnographic Research Simulator (EFRS). The simulated field research exercises take place in an actual ethnographic setting and involves lifelike conditions. It includes exercises and assignments aimed at teaching students cultural navigation (learning to move around in another culture), collecting observational and interview data, participation data, taking field notes, keeping a field diary, and writing field reports. The exercises can be carried out in Spanish or English and/or both languages. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 363B. Formerly known as SBSC 213.

SBSC 264 ASL San Juan Mission Museum Education Project
4 credits
This course is devoted to the creation of a museum education program for the Old Mission of San Juan Bautista. Student projects will center on the development of museum exhibitions, artifact displays, and multimedia instructional materials and educational resources celebrating the bicentennial celebration of the Old Mission. While instruction and project development will center on those methods and procedures entailed in the hands-on creation of a public education program for the Old Mission of San Juan Bautista, the broader learning experience of this project lab will address museum education and docent programming, childrenís multimedia, multicultural education, cultural resource management, and information management in public contexts. Service Learning contributions and community participation will be a mainstay of this learning experience, and students from within the social sciences, the arts and humanities, liberal studies, and teacher education are encouraged to participate in this service learning practicum. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 364SL.

SBSC 269 Ancient Mesoamerica
4 credits
Ancient Mesoamerica is the source of some of the most critically important developments in world agriculture, art, literature, science, technology, and medicine. Mexican civilizations, including those of the Olmec, Teotihuacan, Toltec, Xicalanca, Zapotec, and Mexicanized Maya are the specific focus of this comparative survey. This learning experience will examine the social, cultural, economic, political, and religious underpinnings of these Mesoamerican traditions in comparative and cross-cultural terms and from the perspective of landmark archaeological projects and ethnohistoric interpretations, and their respective significance in the interpretation of the Mesoamerican past. Special consideration is given the technological, social-historical, and ideological foundations of these cultural systems. Mesoamerica, the primary area under consideration, is taken to encompass the region from Zacatecas, Mexico, to the Isthmus of Panama. Upper division credit it available by registering for SBSC 369.

SBSC 272 Race, Ethnicity, Sex and Gender in the Social and Behavioral Sciences
4 credits
This course explores theories and research methods used in the social and behavioral sciences to study race, ethnicity, sex and gender, with special attention paid to the ways these categories are intertwined historically and presently in a variety of geographical settings. The course examine debates over the meaning and value of these categories for the study of human relations and behavior and controversies generated by various genetic and environmental explanations. The emphasis is on reading current scholarship, open discussion of various perspectives and approaches, and collaborative student research projects. SBSC majors may take this course to demonstrate outcomes in MLOs I or II. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 372. Formerly known as SBSC 255.

SBSC 273 Virtual Archaeology and Geographic Information Systems
4 credits
Virtual Archaeology is that area of archaeology that integrates multimedia and information systems in an effort to deploy electronic and computer-assisted technologies in the 3-D visualization, reconstruction, and modeling, and identification of archaeological sites and materials. Because electronic technologies have as of late revolutionized the communication and modeling of scientific information, this course will survey the world of virtual archaeology and visualization, and GIS applications in both prehistoric and historic archaeology. Course applications will center on three primary approaches to the visualization and modeling of archaeological sites and materials, including (a) the use of multimedia applications for the reconstruction of sites and artifacts, (b) the exploration and potential of recent applications in 3-D visualization for archaeological site interpretation, and (c) the use and or potential uses of GPS (Global Positioning System) and GIS (Geographic Information System) applications and technologies in archaeology. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 373.

2 credits

Discussions of different perspectives on how to deal with population growth and resource conservation. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 380. Formerly known as SBSC 276 and SBSC 376.

SBSC 281 Comparative Social History and Political Economy: United States and Vietnam Since 1880
4 credits
This course provides an in-depth comparative analysis of society, political-economy, and culture, in historical perspective for the United States and Vietnam since 1880. Within the context of colonialism, during and after the Vietnam War, this course compares the cultures of Vietnam and the United States, and explores the ways in which these cultures shaped the course of the Vietnam War and subsequent relations between the two countries and their peoples. Students will have the opportunity to meet and interact with Vietnamese scholars who lived through the war, U.S. military personnel of different ethnic backgrounds who fought in Vietnam or who worked as support forces at Fort Ord, and former anti-war/peace activists. Major learning outcomes addressed by this learning experience are Area III, Discipline Specific and Regional Studies.

SBSC 282 Cultural Anthropology: Subfields in the Discipline
4 credits
This course will give consideration to major trends in two or more a sub-fields in cultural anthropology, such as economic anthropology, applied anthropology, advocacy anthropology, and political anthropology. The course is intended to give students and in-depth understanding of the various sub-specializationís in cultural anthropology. The relationship of these fields to other areas of the social sciences will also be examined. Students will do a critical review of the literature in one of these fields, with an emphasis on the current schools of analysis in the particular field. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 382.

SBSC 283A Lost Civilizations of Africa and Eurasia
4 credits
An exploration of the rise and fall of the indigenous states and civilizations of sub-Saharan Africa. While this course places emphasis on the rise of civilization in precolonial or Black Africa, and to a lesser extent, Africaís relations with Eurasia, this learning experience will review the comparative development and contributions of those ancient cultures and civilizations of sub-Saharan Africa and the Mediterranean. Ancient cultures, kingdoms and states including those of dynastic Egypt, Kush, Axum, Jenne, Zimbabwe, the Swahili complex, and Benin and West Africa serve as the primary case studies for this exploration into the archaeology of complex societies. Serves as the learning counterpart to SBSC 283B: Lost Civilizations of the Pacific Rim. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 383A. Formerly known as SBSC 207 and SBSC 307. Option for fulfilling requirement in area studies concentration in SBSC.

SBSC 283B Lost Civilizations of the Pacific Rim
4 credits
This course provides an overview of the evolution and ultimate collapse of the primary ancient states and civilizations of the Pacific Rim. Encompassing that area identified as the Pacific Rim, including Asia and the Americas, this course will review the comparative development and contributions of those ancient cultures and civilizations identified with regions as diverse as Cambodia and Bolivia, and as complex as ancient China, Japan, Peru and Mesoamerica. This course is intended to serve as the learning counterpart of SBSC 283A/383A: Lost Civilizations of Africa and Eurasia. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 383B. Formerly known as SBSC 225 and SBSC 325.

SBSC 288B Applied Research on Mexican Development

The aim of the course is to promote a better understanding of the problems confronting the people of this Latin-American country and their struggle against poverty and economic stagnation. The course will discuss relevant concepts such as development, migration, ethnicity, poverty, and globalization. The course will teach students how to access, and critically analyze information about Mexico on the World Wide Web. Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 388B.

Upper division credit is available by registering for SBSC 388D.

SBSC 289 Indigenous Peoples and Cultures of the Americas
4 credits

Epigraphic or Mayan glyphic studies and research currently underway in the Maya heartland clearly attest to the antiquity and richness of a culture and civilization that evolved and endured in the Mesoamerican tropics for nearly three thousand years. Course content will draw upon an ever expanding world of ìvirtualî software-based glyphic translation and astronomical and calendrical correlation programs created for the purpose of probing directly into the rich literary, artistic, and historical traditions of recently recovered ancient Maya texts and monuments. This learning experience will examine the social, cultural, economic, political, and religious underpinnings of the Maya dynastic tradition in comparative and cross-cultural terms and from the perspective of landmark archaeological projects and glyphic interpretations, and their respective significance in deciphering the larger Maya lifeway. Lower division credit is available by registering for SBSC 248.

SBSC 373 Virtual Archaeology and GIS
4 credits
Virtual Archaeology is that area of archaeology that integrates multimedia and information systems in an effort to deploy electronic and computer-assisted technologies in the 3-D visualization, reconstruction, and modeling, and identification of archaeological sites and materials. Because electronic technologies have as of late revolutionized the communication and modeling of scientific information, this course will survey the world of virtual archaeology and visualization, and GIS applications in both prehistoric and historic archaeology. SBSC 400 BSL Senior Capstone Seminar for Service Learning
4 credits
The Senior Capstone Seminar for Internships and Service Learning is designed to meet the Individual Learning Plan needs of SBS majors. It is also available for students who have negotiated a supervised internship. Students meet in seminar fashion with faculty and community partners in supervised learning activities related to a students' area of specialty, as defined by their ILP. Students will also meet a contracted number of off-campus hours with the agency that sponsors the internship. Activities have included supervised internships with human service agencies, juvenile youth counseling and correctional facilities, community youth centers, or even community computer centers. Students should contact their Academic Advisor and the Capstone facilitator to receive prior approval through an approved ILP and/or senior capstone proposal.
Prerequisite(s): SBSC 300A/B; 308A/B; a 350 course and/or 360 course; or equivalent based on assessment

SBSC 402 Assessment Lab for Graduating Seniors
1 credits
This learning activity is designed for SBSC majors who are entering their first semester of the Senior Capstone. Students must construct a Senior Capstone Committee that includes the capstone supervisor (who instructs the companion SBSC400 and supervises the SBSC401). A student selects an academic advisor based on their area of specialty or focus as determined by their approved ILP. SBS majors must enroll in their academic supervisor's 402 learning experience. The student then meets regularly with the academic advisor for directed research and mentorship. 402 sections vary in their content dependent on the students Individual Learning Plan.
Prerequisite(s): SBSC 300A/B ; SBSC 308A/B; a 350 and/or 360 course; or equivalent based on assessment; an approved Individual Learning Plan and Senior Capstone Proposal must be on record

SBSC 436 Advanced Geographic Information Systems (GIS) and Global Positioning System (GPS) Mapping

SBSC 460 Truth and Method in History:  A Social History Learning Lab
4 credits
This course is a hands-on practicum in advanced qualitative and quantitative methods for research and analysis in social history. Students work both collaboratively and individually on a major research project in social history. Topics vary according to student interests. Classes are conducted in seminar style and emphasize the collaborative development of critical thinking in framing effective research questions and choosing the research methods that are most appropriate to the questions asked. This course is designed especially for SBSC majors who have an emphasis in social history, but is open to all CSUMB students who have the prerequisites. Students who successfully complete this course will have met SBSC MLO II: Research Methodology. Students may link this course to SBSC 212 for upper division credit when offered during the same semester.
Prerequisites: Completed or concurrent enrollment in: 1. any lower division history course from another college or university or completion of the CSUMB U.S. Histories ULR; 2. 1 course in basic statistics and/or Research Method or equivalent based on assessment.
