By Shari René Harder, CSU Monterey Bay

Just as one’s dreams are made up of symbols we attempt to decipher, so is our waking world full of symbols through which we seek to define meaning. Symbols come from our dreams, our imagination, our culture, and the perspectives of others. All such representations are based on our respective beliefs and traditions. For instance, the cross motif is interpreted variously within Christian and Mayan traditions. Whereas the cross or crucifix symbolizes the martyrdom of Christ in the Roman Catholic tradition, among the Maya the cross constitutes the quincunx or center of the cosmos.

The search for meaning in material culture or in religious symbolism represents a way of finding meaning in one’s life, and as cultures converge in our ever-increasing mobile society, a syncretism emerges at both the individual and collective levels of experience. Syncretism represents the hybrid melding of two separate and distinct religious traditions such as Christianity and animism. I will focus on that dimension of syncretism that began when the Spanish explorers first arrived in the New World with their Catholic religious traditions and material culture in tow. This diverged with the cultures of Mesoamerica and reemerged as a hybrid tradition borne of Mestizaje. Each tradition converged as a result of generations of cultural and religious interaction or syncretism.

For the purposes of this website, we will review the results of this convergence of Catholicism and pre-Columbian cultural and religious traditions in the Americas more generally. More specifically, we will explore Catholic folk traditions and material culture among the Mexican immigrants of the California central coast. Mexican folk traditions on the California central coast constitute an interesting mélange of institutional religious traditions and popular folk beliefs. The interplay and intercultural accommodation of both institutionalized and folk traditions by Mexican Catholics serves to reinforce the vibrancy and longevity of the Catholic community on the California central coast.
