Lab Activities

The SBS 224L and 324L Archaeology Lab sections will focus on three to five project efforts that serve to complement and reinforce SBS 224s/324s course content. The projects in question all entail both readings and hands-on or applied learning labs in which you will engage the basics of a specific analytical or experimental procedure. Examples of the sorts of learning modules that we will undertake this semester are described on this portion of the website, and any attendant Internet materials required for the labs will be posted on this page during the course of the semester. Depending on class interest and project schedules, project labs will vary from semester to semester.

Lab Module 1: Artifact and Specimens Analysis

This lab will engage student participants in the hands-on study and interpretation of historic and pre-historic archaeological materials. Specimens and artifacts subjected to analysis and interpretation will be drawn from existing Institute for Archaeology specimens collections recovered from sites currently under study and excavation. From time to time the professor will introduce materials for discussion from his own personal collections.

Lab Module 2: Faunal Analysis and Bone Identification

Drawing on authentic samples of the many faunal or animal bone specimens recovered from the archaeological deposits of Old Mission San Juan Bautista and other area sites, students will be introduced to the means by which bones are recovered, analyzed, and interpreted for cultural meaning.

Lab Module 3: Mission Ceramics and Virtual Collections

Participants in this lab will be introduced to the study and interpretation of ceramics and pottery as this pertains to the California missions. This lab will require the use of a software program titled “Mission Ceramics: A Virtual Type Collection.” Using the program in question, as well as authentic ceramic specimens recovered from archaeological sites currently under study by your professor, you will be given the opportunity to make use of a virtual type collection for the analysis and identification of historic and pre-historic ceramics. See http://archaeology.csumb.edu/Multimedia/MissionCeramicsProject/index.htm for screen shots of the software program used in this lab project.

Lab Module 4: Flintknapping and Stone Tools

This lab entails the production of stone tools from obsidian and related lithic materials. Students will first observe a series of demonstrations by the professor that will result in the creation of a lithic scatter strewn across a gridded tarp. Students will then sample, collect, quantify, and interpret results; after which all participants interested and willing to participate in the hands-on Flintknapping exercise will be afforded the opportunity to learn the basics that underlie the art of stone tool production. See http://archaeology.csumb.edu/Flintknapping/Results/index.html for specimens produced during lab time.

Lab Module 5: Garbology and Modern Material Cultures

Perhaps one of the most interesting aspects of the lab will in fact entail the study of perhaps the most common byproducts and residues of your everyday life…that being your trash and related waste products. Using class volunteers willing to have their trash samples collected and studied on a weekly basis for a period of some five weeks, we will engage in the analysis of household trash for evidence pertaining to dietary practices and behavioral patterns. Participants will be required to have their “anonymous” source complete a questionnaire regarding dietary practices, after which trash samples will be collected, sorted, quantified, and graphed for significant results and observations. See http://archaeology.csumb.edu/Garbology/Group1_5Samples3_14_02.htm for examples of results obtained in one such study.
