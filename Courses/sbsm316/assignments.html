Course Deliverables

Minimum Requirement: Each student will be required to demonstrate that they have developed the fundamental skills and obtained knowledge of those basic technical standards required for the effective visual anthropological and documentary use of the 35mm camera and related film mediums.

Initial Objective: To study the concepts and methods, and work toward the development of those specialized skills, underlying visual anthropology and documentary and or investigative photography, particularly those pertaining to anthropologically-informed photography and studio and lab methods germane to photo-technical imaging.

Ultimate Objective: To produce a theme-based photographic portfolio that serves to document a culture, subculture, or cultural or social theme of contemporary or historical relevance.

Self-Paced Study: Because this learning experience is primarily self-paced, you may opt to complete assigned deliverables well before the end of the term for preliminary assessment (i.e. specifically at mid-term), or for overall assessment at end-term. The only other circumstances that would affect self-paced or accelerated learning styles pertain to the fact that all students are required to complete the same lab and field assignments required of this learning experience. You may however opt to conduct the field studies individually, and at your own convenience, so long as you provide documentation of the field experience and its learning outcomes.

Field Days: Off campus photo excursions will be scheduled on Saturdays at two and three week intervals through the course of the Fall 1996 semester. While it is not required that you participate in the field excursions, such field days provide an opportunity for you to initiate your community study under the guidance of a field mentor. Our specific case study will entail a visual anthropological study of the community of San Juan Bautista, where Professor Mendoza has been engaged in an archaeological, historical, and visual anthropological field study since September of 1995. Lab and Studio Days: On campus lab and studio time will be made available on Tuesday afternoons by arrangement between Professor Mendoza and the students of SBSC 395. Lab and studio demonstrations will take place between 3:00 and 6:00 p.m. Tuesday afternoons by arrangement. Lab and studio days are tentatively set to take place in Building 110, which is the Service Building fronting the front entrance (i.e. glass door entrance) of the SBSC offices in Module 86A. Other locations will be announced as needed. Unlike the Field Days, you are required to attend arranged lab and studio days so that you may be introduced to the range of photographic methods and applications available to the social scientist. Again, if you are able to demonstrate an understanding of the methods involved in lab and studio concepts, you are welcome to submit such documentation in lieu of attending the arranged lab and studio times noted. The following three deliverables represent the primary learning requirements expected of all students in this section:
 

Deliverables Portfolio. All materials produced as a result of assigned readings, journal entries, and photographic assignments based on a cultural or social theme should be submitted for preliminary or midterm assessment no later than November 12th, 1996 (see Portfolio Requirements for format standards). Submission dates will vary according to your self-paced study and learning plan, however, you should target the period between October 15th and November 12th, 1996 as a general guideline for midterm evaluation of your work. While electronic submission is appropriate for midterm portfolios, the final photographic portfolio should be submitted in hard copy.

Lab and Field Journal: Your Lab and Field Journal will be the second major learning requirement of SBSC 395. Said journal should include an ongoing assessment and working document of the subject of your specific case study research and lab and field experience. Journal entries should include dates, locations, technical and creative information regarding photographs produced, and socio-cultural observations pertaining to the community under study. In addition, because assigned readings will serve as the basis for your introduction and skills-building in technical imaging, you are expected to provide documentation that you have in fact completed a body of readings that pertain to your specific area of study (i.e. said readings may be those assigned or those identified as per your specific research effort).

Assigned Readings: On lab and field days, you should come prepared to address issues, discuss concepts, and explore methods described in readings assigned in anticipation of said excursions or labs. While not required, you may wish to consider documenting your readings by way of brief typewritten summaries of those readings that lend themselves most readily to your understanding of the primary concepts and methods of technical imaging and visual anthropology; particularly as this may pertain to your specific field study. Because of the growing body of internet and World Wide Web resources in photography and visual anthropology (e.g. archaeological photography), you may opt to use web site materials as supplementary readings. The scheduled topics will include web site hot-links for your internet survey of currently available sites pertaining to photographic methods in general, and social science applications in particular. Your professor will endeavor to post relevant sites under the topics listed in your on-line class schedule.

Final Photographic Portfolio. The final photographic portfolio will be the primary requirement of this learning experience. Please note that all final portfolios for this learning lab should follow the appropriate format for that specified for the photographic portfolio, and should not include extraneous materials that have not been specifically requested.

Assessment: Primary assessment of your learning experience will be conducted by way of scheduled interviews and portfolio reviews scheduled with your professor at midterm and end term. Evaluation will be based on evidence of your attainment of basic technical proficiencies in photographic methods judged at midterm, and this in turn will be judged in the final analysis on the basis of the quality and content of specific images and critical commentaries and caption information included in the final photographic portfolio.
