Final Term Paper

      Flintknapping is the most common and widespread name for the age-old craft of making
stone tools.  Not just making a stone tool, however it is better said to flake a stone tool.  Most
knappers of the present learn this process through hands on experience and gaining the knowledge
and understanding of why and how stones break or flake.  However, Paleolithic hunters and
gathers knew nothing of shock waves or measuring the angles of the platform in which the stone
would flake.  It was strictly based on skillful eyes and hands that could judge what would work
and what would fail.  For the knappers of our time it is better to have the comprehension of the
basic principles and terminology in order to know why the techniques work or don’t work.  In
other words, what stones are best for flaking, what are the best angles in which to properly strike,
and how much force should be used to get the desired flake.  All of these must be considered
before this process can begin.

      During the early 1800s this process was used tremendously among the British for making
gunflints by breaking long flakes into short pieces.  This was also perceived at the time were
archaeology was just developing into a science and stone tools were starting to be recognized as
ancient artifacts of prehistoric humans. It seems that the ways in which prehistorians vs. modern
day flintnappers went about flaking their stone tools, using there own techniques, and producing
the same types of flakes is learned through experience.  However it is said that a basic
understanding of the tools as well as the process is necessary to utilize the concept of this ancient
craft.

     One necessity for a flintknapper is that the stone have one basic quality, it has to fracture
conchidly in order for the stone not to crack and create other flaws.  To fracture conchidly means
that the fracture surfaces are curved like a cockle shell.  By changing the shape and varying the
angles and force used on the stone, the fracture can be controlled and manipulated to the
flintknappes disirer.  Stones that will fracture conchidly are thus desirable for flaking and share
similar qualities.  The stone has to be brittle and homogeneous, which means that the stone is the
same throughout its shape lacking impurities in texture, planes, cracks, and flaws.  Stones that do
not fit this description will not fracture conchidly but will ultimately crack or break when force is
applied.  The most homogenous stone available is manmade glass or natural volcanic glasses such
as obsidian.  To be brittle means that the rock can be easily broken and not manipulated
extensively without causing it to break.  Therefore the stone must be brittle in order for it to be
flaked and used.

      To accurately be productive as a flintknapper there are several key concepts that need to
be understood.  Cores, which is the piece on the rock in which you strike, and flakes, which are
the pieces which come off after pressure and force have been administered.  The fracture surface
of the flake contains certain features such as the ventral and the dorsal surfaces.  The ventral
meaning belly, and the dorsal meaning back.  The surface at the top of the flake were the initial
blow occurred is called the platform.  This is also the proximal end while the opposite is the distal
end.  If the stone was hit by a hard hammer stone there would likely be a small crushed area called
the point of percussion, most likely on the platform.  Ring cracks, which are small round cracks,
may also appear on the top of the cone.  Just below the interior fracture surface of the flake
swelling will appear while further down ripples, which are the center of the point of percussion,
will also appear.  The force applied to the rock sends waves through its interior which creates
these ripples and flakes.  You can also determine which direction the flake was removed by
inspecting the flake scares, which are curved lines were the flake originally was formed to the
rock.  Radial fissures are also commonly seen on the interior surfaces of flakes.  These fissures are
tiny cracks that are pointed back towards the point were the stone was struck.  Parasitic flake
scares are also left behind and can be seen when a small leaf shaped flake is removed when the
stone is struck.

      All of these features of the interior surface of the flake leave behind a negative indentation
on the core.  There are numerous variations to the size and shape to these features that allow a
person to accurately assume allot about how the flake was originally made.  The exterior surface
tends to show different scars of other flakes.  Some flakes exterior surface may be covered with
cortex, which means it is covered by a rough weathered piece of some other substance.  This
occupancy of cortex indicates that the flake was the first struck from the core.  Besides man made
flakes, natural forces also produce similar flakes that are hard to tell apart.  However, you can
usually tell them apart by noticing patterns.  Natural flakes tend to be random, while man made
flakes are patterned.  In archaeological digs misinterpreting a natural flake for a man made flake is
rare due to the number of hand axes, arrow heads, and other projectile points.  Although some of
the earliest stone tools discovered are so crude that it would be easily mistaken for natural.
Another important factor to the knapper is flake termination’s, which refers to the distal
end of a flake in which there are four main types.  The most common and desirable in the Feather
termination’s, were the flake is removed from the core so smoothly that it produces a sharp
feathered edge.  Hinge termination forms a rounded edge on the end of the flake when the
fracture surface is turned upward.  The step fracture ultimately destroys the flake and the original
force and crack may continue through the core.  An overshoot means the crack continues to the
end of the core and removes part of its end by bending downward.

      One way to create these types of flakes along with others is by using a hard-hammer
method, by striking one rock with another.  This is a very basic principle of knapping and requires
large pieces of material for best results.  The hammer which is used should be lighter than the core
it is striking, because the size of the hammer will affect the size of the flakes produced.  Since
hammer stones will also break it is best to have several handy.  The best type of hammer stones
are stream rounded pebbles that are less brittle and will not likely shatter when being struck
against another rock.  Stones such as chert and obsidian will not make good hammer stones
because with each use the surface of the rock is reduced to an irregular surface.  Rocks that
contain cracks or flaws should not be used.  Different knappers use different stones depending on
their preference, however, the main point is that there should be a noticeable spot or point on the
hamerstone in which to strike.  Over time hammerstones acquire wear patterns that can be easily
depicted.  If used frequently the stone will also develop polished grips were your fingers are
usually placed.

      The concept of knapping is really quit simple.  A good knapper can easily control the force
of the blow being applied, the direction, the point of impact, the shape he/she wants of the core,
and the angles of edges.  With every strike the conditions of the rock change thus the angles and
the pressure being applied must change as well.  When you first start knapping it is hard to
determine what you are actually doing.  In order to completely understand how to knapp you
must understand how to control the variables which create the flakes.  These variables include
platform depth, angle of the blow, exterior platform angle, and force of the blow.  To get a large
flake more force must be applied as well as a greater platform depth.  Length is determined by the
exterior platform angle as well as the different degrees in which the rock is struck.  The most
important thing to remember is the exterior platform angle, the angle in which you strike the rock,
and also the shape of the exterior surface were the flake breaks from.  It is common for beginning
knappers to hit straight down on the rock, this crushes the rocks platform and produces cones
that also ruin the platform.  To produce useful flakes it is best to strike the rock at an angle that is
less the 90 degrees but greater than 35-45 degrees.  To hit the core at these angles it is best if you
steady the rock against your leg allowing only your striking hand to be in motion.  Moving the
core forces less accuracy and will probably ruin the angle of the blow.

      The key to a successful knapping lies within the platforms of the rock.  It is very important
that the knapper understand and notice the platforms before they strike the stone.  The platform
surface should be clean, free of ridges, soft cortex, or any flaws or irregularities.  A bad platform
can usually be saved if it is less than 90 degrees by detaching another flake and using the face of
the other edge as a platform.  This will save your platform and make your stone bifacial in the
process.  One of the easiest things to control is the exterior platform angle by facetting, trimming,
and rubbing.  Facetting removes flakes from the platform surface which is a useful way to remove
irregularities.  This process also changes and increases the exterior platform angle which in turn
increases the length of the flake.  Trimming removes all small flakes from the exterior surface of
the core which can change the platform angle.  This process is useful because it removes platform
edges and reduces the change of crushing the platform.  Rubbing is done with the hamerstone by
rubbing it across the edge of the platform.  This also removes thin edges and overhangs that could
damage the effects of the flakes.  Rubbing also dulls sharp edges of the platform and allows it
easier to produce flakes.  All of these techniques help stabilize the platform which makes it easier
for the knapper.

      Pressure flaking is another technique used by knappers which uses pressure to remove
portions of the stone.  This technique is used with a flaker, or a deer antler, and instead of hitting
the stone you simply press the antler against the core causing small pieces to break as a result of
added pressure.  The platform used for this technique is not a flat surface but a ridge.  The angle
used is still less than 90 degrees, however, the angle is formed against the edge itself and is
applied almost parallel to the centerplane.  The ideal platform is just above the face in which you
intend to remove a flake and slightly below the other.  A bifacial edge is also ideal but takes more
time to construct.  A helpful technique within this method is called shearing, which involves
crushing flakes off the edge with the side of the antler.  Another technique called buffering, uses
the flaker in one direction away from the face were you are flaking to pull of different flakes.  This
is also a way of dulling the edges of the core.  Pressure flaking is also a good way to create
notches in your flakes, such as in projectile points.  This is done by repeatedly removing edges
from both sides of the face which creates deep and narrow notches.  The tip of the antler or flaker
is the most commonly used for this type of technique.  In order to create an ideal notch downward
pressure should be administered and the platform should be at the centerplane.  Small broad flakes
will be removed when this technique is used properly.  To sum up the pressure flaking technique
one should remember a few essential methods.  The pressure is applied to the edge of the piece,
which is the platform, and should be of an angle less than 90 degrees.  The flaker should be placed
on the edge with force applied in the direction the flake is to be removed.  Edges should be dull
enough to crush and biface all thick edges and right angles.  The last thing to remember is that the
platform should be below the centerplane of the piece you are working with.
