Project Report, Experimental Archaeology: Garbology






Garbological Analysis and Conclusions Regarding Subject O.

Collection and quantification of Subject O.’s garbage began on December 3 and ended December 6. At first, Subject O. surrendered his garbage without hesitation, but once finding out it was being used to study his behavior, he began to filter his garbage before giving it to me. Thus, there are only one to two days’ worth of honest data. He submitted the garbage from both December 5 and 6 combined in one bag; thus, there are only three days’ worth of collection quantification listed.

After analyzing the leavings of Subject O., it is possible to reach some strong conclusions about his behaviors and his environment. Foremost, there was no evidence of any kind relating to the consumption of any fruits or vegetables, fresh, frozen or otherwise. The only food-items found were either pre-packaged or canned, some specifically excluding vegetable material (i.e., chili without beans), with the sole exclusion being that of apple juice. Thus, it can be hypothesized that Subject O. does not consider fruits or vegetables to be part of his diet, except in juice-form.

The continuation of the analysis revealed that in addition to his canned food, Subject O. eats a great deal of starchy foods, through cheese-flavored and rice-flavored crackers. This can be determined from the discovery of several large-capacity containers of these items. Apparently, he especially prefers the cheese-flavored, due to the discovery of the 3 lb. container with accompanying bags. He also appears to enjoy sweet foods: his consumption of soft-drinks, specifically Pepsi-Cola, is extremely high (eleven containers appeared in a single analysis), while evidence of chewing gum and gum wrappers occurred in every analysis.

This individual would seem to be the owner of both a PC and a platform game console, due to the discovery of the packaging of multiple accessories. On the second collection analysis, there were a pair of two-pack ink-cartridge containers for a high-end computer printer. On the third analysis, the cellophane from a Sony Playstation game was found, along with the packaging of a game-controller and several discarded recordable CDs. Mail, coupons and newspapers (specifically: PC Paper) relating to software and computer items confirmed this conjecture.

Despite his eating habits, Subject O. is apparently quite concerned with personal hygiene. On every occasion of analysis, evidence of this was made apparent, especially on the second and third analyses, with a travel toiletry kit, two packages of floss and the discarding of an ordinary travel toothbrush in favor of a high-end consumer toothbrush.

Subject O. may have been raised in an environment where neither food nor money are scarce, as he discards with great profligacy and never recycles, neither is he picky about completely finishing his meals.  Cans of chili and ravioli invariably had enough residue for a few spoonfuls more, discarded bags of crackers were never completely empty, pizza pieces left half-eaten, etc. Indeed, plastic utensils appeared in every single can, most fastened tightly to the bottom in congealed sauce. Other items were discarded even before use; an unused travel toothbrush, for example. Finally, all three of the collection analyses showed evidence of shopping having occurred, with expenditures between $60.00 and $150.00; discovery of receipts and price tags confirmed this.

On a final note, discovery of notes about ancient Japanese weaponry and the package of imported Japanese rice crackers would seem to suggest Subject O. to be either of Japanese descent, or to have an interest in Japanese culture.

I chose to categorize the data into several sub-groups, as is shown by the table above.  I thought that perhaps the best option would be to compare non food-related items (i.e., CDs, video game materials, etc.) versus food and other food-related  items in order to show exactly how much of the garbage made up diet.  Secondly, I thought recyclables could give clues as to his economic status (as mentioned in the project proposal), especially because a vast amount of Subject O.'s leavings could have been recycled.  Finally, I thought perhaps an overall picture would be useful for purposes of comparison.

On the subject of the experimental archaeological tactic, garbology, it would seem to be a useful tool in determining the habits, attitudes and interests of a person or group of people. The amount of evidence discovered from the leavings of Subject O. does exactly that: it allows the investigator to come up with good hypotheses and assumptions relating to his nature. However, it is important, as with any scientific endeavour, that the data be quantified carefully. Careless grouping or exclusion of data can quickly skew the resulting picture.
