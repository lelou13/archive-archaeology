Archaeology Project Proposal



There has long been a debate between historians and archaeologists. Historians gain an understanding of the past through the collection, copy, translation and analysis of written records. Archaeologists, on the other hand, study the past by surveying an area directly and studying the leavings of their material culture left in and on the soil, in an attempt to piece together a behavioral picture of the people. Historians claim there is little place for archaeology in a historical venue, as its results are mostly theory and conjecture; written records are concrete evidence. Unfortunately, few human cultures actually developed writing, thus only a small percentage of human history has been written down. Furthermore, due to the work of the elements, even fewer written records have survived to the present. This leaves the historian floundering while the archaeologist steps into the limelight. Yet the question still remains: does the study of ancient leavings tell anything about the people who left them?

One way to find out whether archaeology is useful is to study modern trash to see if it provides clues as to the lifestyle of a person or group of people. This process is called experimental archaeology; specifically in this case, garbology. Determination of this is not just from the trash; proper questions need to be answered in order to determine the nature of that person. For example: "What does this person eat, and how do they eat it?" Diet can tell a lot about a person or people, especially in today’s society. Food waste and food packaging would be the debitage of a modern person’s diet. If a person eats a lot of take-out and pre-packaged food, one could guess that he or she has a fairly stable income, since those types of food tend to cost more. Conversely, if a person eats a lot of generic or store-brand foods, supplemented with produce, one might guess that he or she is on a lesser step of the economic ladder.

Another question might be: "How does this person live? What kinds of things do they do?" The types of non food-related packaging materials a person discards can give insight into what types of things that person collects, maintains or uses. Paper waste can give clues as to what type of work he or she does, or what kind of student he or she is. Other miscellaneous items round out the picture.

Finally, other evidence can round out the picture and perhaps provide clues into questions that may not yet have been asked. For example, postulations can be made about the person’s economic status and their attitudes when compared with their recycling waste. If a person does not recycle, perhaps it is because they are careless, or perhaps they are too financially well-endowed to worry about recyclable redemption value. If a person does recycle, perhaps it is because they care about the environment, or they need the redemption money from such items.

It is through garbology I plan to prove the feasibility of archaeology as a historical science. Using the above strategies and theories, I will collect, quantify and analyze trash from a neighbor. With the resulting data, I hope to create an accurate picture of that person’s life, habits and behaviors.
