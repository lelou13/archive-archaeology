End-Term Self Assessment
 
 

After taking into consideration the past semester, I think that I have done fairly well in this class. Although I may not have attended every session, I feel have learned the essence of the archaeological process; the science behind the shovels, so to speak. I’ve learned the ability to apply a logical method to my work. I’ve learned to be an observer and a surveyor. I’ve picked through trash, I’ve counted rocks, I’ve spent hours upon hours quantifying data—hell, I’ve even managed to learn how to bash a couple of sticks and rocks together and come out with a useable tool.

I’ve come to understand archaeology as being the science of creating something from nothing. Almost magically, an archaeologist can take into the laboratory a few layers of soil, measurements of scattered rocks, collections of potsherds and then tell you all about the people who lived on a particular squat of land thousands of years ago. The effect is humbling, because the trappings of a material culture—and humans have maintained a material culture ever since they started lugging sticks and rocks around—can tell someone all about you, even long after you’ve been dead. The magic is science; a prestidigitation of rocks and flakes, tables and charts, graphs and data into a feasible theory of human history. Indeed, there is a sense of magic as the last piece of evidence falls into place. After I’d spent hours collecting and quantifying garbage, the last few little things came together to provide a crystal-clear picture of the behaviors of the fellow I was studying.

After all the bloodied knuckles, gashed fingers, obsidian splinters, garbage swill and bloodshot eyes, what can be said? Archaeology is indeed a science, but a down-home, get-your-hands-dirty science. Yet, in all its muddiness, both literal and conjectural, there is an elegance. I can state from personal experience that one man’s trash truly is another man’s treasure—especially if that man is an archaeologist. Sweep away the dirt and you find history. Move aside the clods of debris and you will find civilization. It is to science what taking high tea out of a pewter coffee-mug is to fine dining. It may not be pretty, but it gets the job done.
