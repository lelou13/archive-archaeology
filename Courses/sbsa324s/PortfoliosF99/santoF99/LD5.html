1. During the search for the "Lost Spanish Mission" David Hurst Thomas and his team used many different tools and procedures. They started by using the transect sampling strategy. He and his team walked a series of 31 east-west transects, each 100 meters wide. The survey turned up 135 mostly unrecorded archaeological sites. Then they investigated each site with tow or more 1-meter-square test units. They excavated more than 400 test pits. With the test pits they were able to see if there was anything in the area that was worth a total excavation or if they should move on. They next used a gasoline-powered posthole digger and they excavated hundreds of round holes. The machine can dig a 3-foot-deep hole in less than a minute. The power auger throws up a sample of dirt to be hand-sifted for artifacts. They then used a proton precession magnetometer. The magnetometer measures the strength of magnetism between the earth's magnetic core and a sensor controlled by the archaeologist. Once hundreds of these readings have been done then a computer plotter can generate a magnetic contour map reflecting both the shape and the intensity of magnetic anomalies beneath the ground surface. There were many advantages gained from the use of the tools and the procedures done. One main one was that they were using, for the most part non-destructive methods, therefore preserving the land until they were fairly sure where they wanted to excavate the main site. Also by trying different tools and techniques they opened themselves up to many possible sites and discoveries they otherwise would have missed because they only used one or two types of tools and procedures. I believe that the methods employed were successful in that they found what they were looking for in a timely fashion and they did not destroy any land in the process of searching for the "Lost Spanish Mission."

2. Thomas and his crew first divided St. Catherine’s Island into 31 transects and these horizontal imaginary lines that transected the island. They were used so that the researchers could conceptualize their research area. By dividing the island into smaller portions, they were able to assign different areas to different people, making them responsible for that small area. They also made the work areas realistic and not seem like to large to excavate completely. The rationalization of being able to conceptualize the research area are why archaeologists dig square holes. Test pits tend to be one-meter squares that can descend into the ground for many feet. Researchers are able to add a third dimension to their findings when they make these pits, i.e.; front to back, side to side, and top to bottom. These pits also give the archaeologist an idea as to how the artifacts relate to each other within the area.

3. Ethnoarchaeology, Garbology, and Experimental Archaeology all add to the concept of Middle Range Research. Basically Middle Range Research is where archaeologists look at what they know about the cultures of yesterday and compare them to today's culture. It creates a link between the cultures of today and yesterday. Ethnoarchaeology is where the archaeologist goes into the particular culture and lives as they live and they make assumptions about the generations that have come before them. They see how the people are living now and they relate that to the way that they lived in the past. Also they get information from the people of the culture. They learn some of the oral history that has been passed down through the ages. Garbology allows the archaeologist to learn about the culture through the garbage left behind. From the garbage they can learn about the diet from the bones, seed, and shells from food. They can also learn about the clothes and tools used from the broken tools that were thrown away and pieces of clothing and material. Just little remnants of food, clothing, and tools can provide information about how the people of that culture lived. Experimental Archaeology is where the archaeologists try doing things as the people from the past might have done them. One example is the mound builders experiment. A group of archaeologists and students create a mound using the techniques that were evident from the excavated mounds. They learned about the process by going through it. It allows the archaeologist to experience things from a different perspective. This allows them to see first hand how they build or made things. All of these provide insight into how past generations lived and what connections there are to today's culture. It provides the missing link.
