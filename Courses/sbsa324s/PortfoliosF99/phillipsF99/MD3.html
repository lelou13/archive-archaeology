Major Deliverable 3: Field Journal
SBSC 224
Fall 1999

Day 1 - September 30, 1999
- On this day we began to set up the environment for the stone tools experiment. First we taped down a tarp which was marked with a grid, each square approximately 6” X 6”. We were instructed to don leather gloves, protective eyewear and to each obtain a piece of leather, also for protection along with various tools for the project. The tools included a hammerstone along with antlers for soft percussion.
- Each of us then selected a piece of obsidian to work with. These varied in size and shape. For the next hour and a half, we participated in the craft of creating stone tools by hitting our obsidian core with the tools we had selected, with the knowledge we had gained from a video the previous class meeting time along with instructional handouts on flintknapping.

Day 2 – October 7, 1999
- Today, we gathered the debitage. To do this, we each picked, at random, two or three slips of paper, each referring to a square on the grid. These were our assigned squares for analysis. We were to gather the debitage in each of the squares, keeping them separate. A chart was indicated on the board as to how to categorize the pieces of debitage. The categories were as follows: <1cm, 1-2cm, 3-4cm, 5-7cm, 7-9cm,  and 9-12cm. We then weighed each piece and reported our findings to Dr. Mendoza, who recorded them into a computer. My grid squares were B13 and J11.
- The weights of the pieces I found in B3 are as follows: <1 cm: <200 items - 4.5 grams, 1-2 cm: 28 items -  4.5 grams, 2-3 cm: 6 items -  3.5 grams, 3-4 cm: 4 items – 6.5 grams, 4-5 cm: 1 item – 5.2 grams, 5-7 cm: no items of this size, 7-9 cm: 1 item – 251.7 grams. I categorized 5 additional items found in B3 separately because they were made of a different type of material: jasper, rather that obsidian as the others. These items weighed as follows: <1cm: 3 items – 0.2 grams and 1-2 cm: 2 items – 0.3 grams.
- The weights of the pieces I found in J11 are as follows: <1 cm: <100 items – 0.7 grams, 1-2 cm: 2 items – 0.7 grams, 2-3 cm: no items of this size, 3-4 cm: no items of this size, 4-5 cm: 2 items – 20.3 grams.

Day 3 – October 21, 1999
Today, we each chose a new piece of obsidian to work with. My results were: two partially formed arrowheads.

Day 4 – November 4, 1999
Today, our work area expanded into another section of the lab as we were filling up the room with debitage. This day, I tried working quite a bit with an antler. I tried different sizes of hammerstones as well. One of the flakes I came up with appeared to be a fairly decent scraper.

Day 5 – November 11, 1999
Today, I decided to work in a self-guided manner of experimentation to see the size, shape and direction of flakes as I worked, rather than try to imitate what I had learned in the readings and the video. Since my skill didn’t seem to be improving too much, I thought I’d act as if I were a Neanderthal woman starting from scratch and creating a tool necessary for my survival. I learned a few tips to better shape the pieces I was trying to create, but all in all, I didn’t come up with anything too fantastic.

Day 6 – December 2, 1999
Notes: Cores can be exhausted by too much hammering, at which time a new core should be sought.
PF – primary flaking: >80% cortex/dorsal surface – stone minimally worked
SF – secondary flaking: >1% <80%
TF – tertiary flaking: 0% cortex – stone is usually being reduced to arrowheads
UF – unutilized flakes – stone hasn’t been used, marginal retouch or attrition flaking
Culture: humans have had something to do with the modification of the material
Use – sickle sheen – evidence of use – appeared on obsidian when it was used to cut wheat; pollen left on edge of tool. Wheat brushing the edge also polished it.
Mesopotamia – now modern day Iran and Iraq – turned into a desert 10,000 years ago by human manipulation
Obsidian – Neolithic Period – New Stone Age – Agricultural era in Middle East
Today we used the tools we had created to cut different materials such as wood, cloth, leather and antlers. We then put them in bags which we labeled for later observation. We put each piece under a microscope to observe the edge used to cut with. We drew diagrams of the edges showing what we observed under the microscope.
