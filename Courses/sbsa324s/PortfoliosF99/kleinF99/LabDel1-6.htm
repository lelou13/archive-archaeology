Author: John Klein.- Subject: Flintknapping Video.- 3 of 16Records Found.

Date: 9/29/99.- Time: 12:24:07 AM.- Entry Num: 257

"Flintknapping" was the title of the video that we watched in class today. Bruce

Bradley took us through the process of creating different stone tools to help in the

killing, skinning, carving, and hide preparation involved in hunting a bison. Using both

small and large antlers, and different sized stones, Bruce created tools using

pressure and percussive flaking. Pressure flaking was used to create a serrated

edge, as well as for the delicate sharpening and notching procedures. Bruce used a

notched antler and a small antler tip for pressure flaking. For percussive flaking,

Bruce used the blunt end of a larger antler and two different sized stones. The larger

of these stones was used to flake off a large piece of obsidian from the

"motherchip." The smaller stone was used to thin down and shape the flake so that

the antlers could be used. I think that a short video like this does a great job of

introducing students to flintknapping, but can hardly do justice to hundreds of

thousands of years of stone tool craftsmanship. An accurate and in-depth

documentary of stone tools throughout human history would be a mammoth

semester-long undertaking. This video did precisely what it was supposed to do,

stimulate interest in flintknapping.

Author: John Klein.- Subject: Chichen.- 5 of 16Records Found.

Date: 9/22/99.- Time: 12:44:55 AM.- Entry Num: 224

The ancient city of Chichen Itza on the Yucatan Peninsula in Mexico affords many

examples of sculpture, architecture, and daily life of an ancient civilization. Early

scholars and archaeologists like Augustus LePlongeon came into these sites in the

mid 1800s to look for leftover artifacts of the elite. In the process they described what

the found and theorized about the origins of not only the artifacts that they found but

the motivation of everyday events depicted in the city. The quality of the excavated

carvings in the 1800s made early scholars very fortunate observers of Mayan culture.

Today, we are still lucky enough to see these carvings, but not in the same splendor

as they were 100 years ago. Erosion and fading due to sun, wind, and rain have

faded the paint and physical intensity, but not the impression that they leave on an

inquisitive mind. This site is important to the field of archaeology in that it makes a

connection between ancient and contemporary Maya. Cultural swapping, such as the

transposition of similar buildings, can be interpreted to show us patterns of migration

and emigration.

Research design are the methodologies an archaeologist follows while in the field.

These methods vary from dig site to dig site, depending on such variables as soil

type and type of artifacts present. One such methodology would be to excavate two

inches of soil at a time. As the soil is removed in two inch increments, findings can

be carefully logged in a systematic fashion. The methods that are used should ideally

best help answer the research questions. The research questions should address

what aspect of the dig that the researcher wishes to be answered. For example, if

stone axes are being excavated in a temple floor in Chichen, then a research

question such as "What purpose did the stone axe play in the everyday life for the

Chichen natives?" Another research question may seek to find patterns in

architechtural design throughout MesoAmerica. "What are the the similarities

between structures, and how did they occur?" might be an appropriate research

question in this case. If a large area must be screened for potential dig sites, then

site location is an important part of the archaeological process. Site location

involves choosing one area over another. Such decisions could be made on such

variables as proximity to water, elevation (for safety, and protection against invasion)

, as well as by proximity to vegetation and animal life. These factors are all very

important to daily life and could dictate whether or not a group of people would live in

a particular area. Site location was an important factor in the crescent rockshelter.

Dr. Mendoza was involved in a dig in an area that had previously been surveyed and

deemed worthless. Site location was what let Dr. Mendoza find significant

archaeological artifacts in the land while it offered nothing to previous excavators.

Looking at soil samples (soil analysis) can help predict the likelihood of a significant

finding, as well. Soil of a certain color can indicate human remains, bodily as well as

garbolic. A soil sample can also serve as a type of dating system, to get, if nothing

else, an approximate time period of the artifacts being excavated.

1. David Hurst Thomas and his crew used several different methods in order to find

mission Santa Catalina on the island of St. Catherines Island in Georgia. The first

task that these archaeologists faced was the location of the mission. Vegetation had

overtaken much of the island and had left no visible traces of the settlement above

ground. The team used random transect sampling in its first attempt to find the site of

the mission. This involved separating the island into 31 east to west transects, with

members of the team walking a percentage of these imaginary lines looking for

potential sites. This method reduced the search area to the area of 30 football fields,

which was much more focused than the whole island, but much too large to be

thoroughly excavated. In order to reduce the area of the site, the crew employed the

use of a gasoline power auger, basically a machine that digs holes. Artifacts found in

the soil piles left by the auger were mapped in order to let the archaeologists know

where high concentrations of artifacts were hidden. Using this method, the potential

dig site was reduced from 30 football fields to 3 acres. The great advantage of the

auger was its ability to quickly dig holes and leave the piles of soil in easily

searchable "doughnuts." The crew then began to utilize remote sensing in order to

find the main structures of the mission. Since they expected to find "wattle-and-daub"

style buildings, they used a proton precession magnometer. This device would be

able to locate the iron found in mud daub plaster. A "magnetic contour map" of the

site was created using the magnometer, which hinted at three particular areas within

the site. When these areas where excavated, a well casing, domestic artifacts, and

the "downed wall of a much larger mission building" were found. One tremendous

advantage of using a magnometer is that no actual digging needs to be done. This

can be an important technique for exploration in physically and politically sensitive

areas. I think that the techniques and devices used by Thomas and his crew fulfilled

his mission objectives beautifully. The Santa Catalina Mission digs also offers and

example of how archaeological techniques have matured and changed in the past

hundred years. 2. When Thomas and his crew first divided St. Catherines Island into

31 transects; they were bringing the overgrowth into the 21st century. These

horizontal imaginary lines that "transected (hence the name)" the island were used

as a tool in order for the researchers to better conceptualize their research area. By

dividing the island into smaller portions, they were able to delegate responsibility to

different people for different areas of the island. They were also able to further

reduce the island into more manageable work areas. These same rationalization of

being able to conceptualize the research area are why archaeologists "dig square

holes." Test pits tend to be one-meter squares that can descend into the ground for

many feet. By making the pits square, researchers are able to add a third dimension

to their findings, "front to back, side to side," and "top to bottom." This is an

improvement over finding artifacts later by sifting in that one can see specifically

where the objects were in relation to each other. These square pits are much more

time consuming and generally more fruitful than the results that an auger can give, as

they should be. Augers are used to locate the areas where square pits where

excavation subsequently occurs. 3. In the words of Thomas, middle-range research

is "linking archaeological data with the relevant aspects of human behavior that

produced it." Ethnoarchaeology does this same thing by observing firsthand similar

behaviors that are related in either content or outcome. Observed relationships can

be applied to a historical context and attempt to explain confusing phenomenon.

These frameworks based on facts (theories) can contribute to larger bodies of

knowledge by providing scenarios to explain cultural events. Garbology focuses

bodies of knowledge concerning living individuals by letting us see exactly what

people do, versus what they SAY they are doing. Similarly, garbolic archaeology can

let us know what was going on during a time period in the past. If nothing else, we

can GUESS what was going on…or what continues to go on. Experimental

archaeology reminds me of participant observation, in that a researcher participates

in similar cultural activities as the subject of inquiry, and makes observations

pertaining to those activities. Gaining insights into hunting mammoths by hunting

elephants with similar equipment is one example of this.
