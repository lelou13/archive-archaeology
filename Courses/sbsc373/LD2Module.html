Lab Deliverable 2:
Problem-Oriented Learning Modules

Directions: The "Problem-Oriented Learning Module" provides an opportunity to work with the "problem-based" approached to learning.  Generally defined, the "problem-based" approach to learning essentially entails working from a problem or research question through to the facts and interpretations related to the problem originally posed.  In this way, students and users of educational multimedia are provided a problem-based scenario which requires interpretation and solution.  The advantage to the "problem-based" or "project-based" approach to learning is that it requires direct engagement and interaction with the subjectmatter under consideration.

    Thomas Patterson's (1994) text The Theory and Practice of Archaeology: A Workbook provides a problem oriented approach to the study of archaeology.  In thinking through this assignment (LD1), and that of Major Deliverable 1 (Project Prospectus), be prepared to draw on the work of Patterson.  Because the Patterson text provides examples of archaeological materials and settlements amenable to particular types of interpretation and study, you should use this text as a guideline for framing potential questions and developing a problem-based approach to the interpretation of the site of Old Mission San Juan Bautista.

    Unlike textbook learning, the "problem-based" approach requires both analysis and assessment of actual or anticipated outcomes.  The purpose of this assignment will be to have you work toward the development of a problem-based "learning module" for the project site under consideration.  Drawing on the following categories and domains of inquiry, prepare an outline of potential "problem sets" or "exercises" that might be undertaken at the Old Mission of San Juan Bautista:

 1.    The Scientific Method in Archaeology and or History (principles and procedures for the systematic pursuit of knowledge).

    a.    Recognition and formulation of a problem.
    b.    Collection of data through observation and experiment.
    c.    Formulation and testing of hypotheses.

 2.    Cultural Interpretation (e.g. trade, social organization, conflict)

    a.    Deduction: "deriving a conclusion by reasoning...in which the conclusion about particulars follows necessarily from general or universal premises" (Websters 10th, 1994:301)

    b.    Induction: "inference of a generalized conclusion from particular instances" (Websters 10th, 1994:594)

 3.    Natural Environment.

    a.    Influence on human settlement
    b.    Influence on cultural forms

 4.    Cultural Geography, Setting, and History.

    a.    Demography and settlement
    b.    Limiting conditions of human settlement and cultural dispersion

 5.    Research and Methods.

    a.    Analysis of Materials (e.g. bone, shell, stone, etc.)
    b.    Analysis of Sites (e.g. camps, towns, etc.)

 6.    Lab and or Field Methods.

    a.    Archaeological excavations
    b.    Field reconnaissance
    c.    Froth flotation/sifting of soils

 7.    Stratigraphy and Soils.

 8.    Cultural and Natural Features.

 9.    Material Culture and its Interpretation.

10.    Plant and Animal Remains.

    a.    Inferences from animal remains
    b.    Inferences from plant remains
    c.    Inferences from human remains

 11.    Chronometric Methods and Interpretations (i.e., Dating methods)
