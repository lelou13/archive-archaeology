SBSC 100A

Introduction to the Social and Behavioral Sciences

Topics and Themes

This learning experience represents the first part of a two part, two semester overview of the social sciences. This portion of the two semester introduction to the social sciences explores primary themes drawn from anthropology and archaeology, cultural geography and demography, and social history and related non-Western narrative traditions.

Subthemes explored include the interdisciplinary (or cross-disciplinary) threads that unite the whole of the social sciences. The principal frame of reference for this learning experience centers on how social theory and explanations of the human condition contribute to framing human social phenomena and processes in ways that are researchable and interpretable, and that allow researchers to arrive at explanatory models and assessments of human behavior based on those observable and verifiable cultural, social, historical, geographic, and economic and political phenomena that frame those same dimensions taken to represent the appropriate and bounded areas of study germane to the social sciences.

The following themes will serve as the primary areas of study for this learning experience. Each area will serve as the framework around which this course is structured, and will in turn provide the windows through which your professors will guide you through a multitude of worlds, both past and present, that frame the human experience. Each faculty member, in his and her turn, will provide students with the theoretical tools and perspectives necessary for navigating these varied areas of study and reflection.

Primary Theme
  • The Study of Migration and Human Interaction

Secondary Themes
  • The Social Science of Contemporary Problems and their Origins
  • The Cultural, Social, and Geographic Landscapes of the Human Condition
  • Doing Social Science Research in the Post-Modern World

We shall also explore how social science theories, applications, and social facts are themselves limited by a number of fundamental factors when applied to specific social science field conditions, social landscapes, ethical considerations, and political realities. Further, we shall demonstrate how social science methods, and individual ideological and cultural orientations, serve to limit the body of social and behavioral science theories available tosocial scientists examining specific policies and researchable questions.
