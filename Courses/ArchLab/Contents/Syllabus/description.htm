Course Description

SBS 332: World Archaeology - 4.0 credits [CRN 21137]

Description: Explores the origins and consequences of the Paleolithic through Neolithic periods of human cultural development that underlie the rise of civilization and the origins of the modern state. The domestication of plants and animals, early village life, the development of metallurgy and the arts, the origins of writing and the earliest dynastic traditions, and the rise of urbanism and the state serve as the primary cultural backdrop. Formerly known as SBSC 332.

Teacher Credential: This course is designed for SBS and Liberal Studies majors who require instruction and study in world history as per the K-12 History/Social Science Content Standards in effect at this time. As such, your professor will provide a systematic overview of that content that seeks to document the origins, evolution, and development of world civilizations in each of their respective hearth areas.

Note: Beginning with the Spring 2005 offering of SBS 332, this course will depart somewhat from traditionalist approaches to the study of ancient civilizations. We will, in effect, endeavor to undertake a course of study that will explore the realities behind the rise and fall of ancient civilizations by way of studying what both the scientific and pseudo-scientific communities have to say about such topics as the origins of the first cities, trans-oceanic contacts and migrations, sunken continents, ancient technologies, and related popular attempts to account for primeval complexity and technological sophistication. To that end, we will make use of both Kenneth Feder's Frauds, Myths, and Mysteries, and Brian Fagan's The Seventy Great Mysteries of the Ancient World.


Required Texts
Ryan, Donald P. 1999. The Complete Idiot's Guide To Lost Civilizations. Indianapolis, Indiana: Alpha Books.

Fagan, Brian M. 2001. The Seventy Great Mysteries of the Ancient World: Unlocking the Secrets of Past Civilizations. New York: Thames & Hudson, Inc.

Feder, Kenneth L. 2002. Frauds, Myths, and Mysteries: Science and Pseudoscience in Archaeology. Fourth Edition. Boston: McGraw-Hill/Mayfield.


Note: Additional readings will be used to supplement the primary texts, and will include both Reserve Desk readings, Internet reports, and related sources.
