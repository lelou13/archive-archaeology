Primary Project Theme

Themes: Primary themes center on museum conservation methods, exhibition design and public education in local museum settings, Hispanic and Native American arts and ethnohistory, public archaeology, and museum anthropology studies.

Case studies: (a) the process underlying the planning, development, and fabrication of museum education exhibitions, (b) museum renovation, conservation, and the interpretation and display of the artifacts and project materials recovered from the Old Mission of San Juan Bautista, (c) museum development and renovation employing art historical, archaeological and ethnohistorical collections and virtual simulations and related electronic media, (d) the lab and field contexts of theory, method and practice in the social sciences, (e) archaeology, social history, and museum education within the context of the community under study, and the (f) the formation and contextualization of alternative histories.

Primary Course Themes and Objectives

This learning experience explores primary themes drawn from anthropology and archaeology, modern material culture studies, ethnohistory and social history and the related study of non-Western narrative traditions. Subthemes explored include the interdisciplinary (or cross-disciplinary) threads that unite the whole of the social sciences (e.g. the relationship of public archaeology and social history). The principal frame of reference for this learning experience centers on how social theory and explanations of the human condition contribute to framing human social phenomena and processes in ways that are researchable and interpretable, and that allow researchers -- particularly archaeologists and social historians -- to arrive at explanatory models and assessments of human behavior based on those observable and verifiable cultural, social, historical, geographic, and economic and political phenomena that frame those same dimensions taken to represent the appropriate and bounded areas of study germane to the social sciences; particularly as these dimensions of human behavior are studied by archaeologists, museum anthropologists and social historians.

The following themes will serve as the primary areas of study for this learning experience. Each area will serve as the framework around which this field experience is structured, and in turn, will serve to provide the window through which your professor will guide you through a multitude of worlds, both past and present, that frame the human experience as expressed in the social and cultural history of an early California community. Students will be exposed to a broad array of methods and theoretical tools and perspectives necessary for navigating those social and cultural studies germane to museum anthropology, public archaeology, and social history.

The Writing of Alternative Histories

As we approach the subject of this study, be mindful of those procedures brought to bear by social scientists intent on exploring and testing social science theories, technical applications, research problems, and social "facts" which are themselves limited by a number of fundamental factors when examined in terms of specific social science field conditions, social landscapes, ethical considerations, and political realities. Further, we shall demonstrate how social science methods specific to archaeology, and individual ideological and cultural orientations in turn serve to limit the body of social and behavioral science theories and interpretations available to social scientists examining specific policies and researchable questions.
