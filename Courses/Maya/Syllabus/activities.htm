Lab Activities

Where pertinent, SBS 248/348 option labs will draw upon resources related to between three and five hands-on activities that complement course content. The labs in question entail both readings and hands-on or applied activities in which you will engage the basics of a specific analytical or interpretive exercise. Examples of the types of learning modules that we will undertake this semester are described on this portion of the website, and any attendant Internet materials required for the labs will be posted on this page during the course of the semester. Depending on class interest and in-class activities, project labs will vary from semester to semester. See the Cuneiform Lab for a recent example of one such lab activity.

Lab Module 1: The Art History Lab

This lab will engage student participants in the indirect and hands-on study, identification, and interpretation of ancient Mesoamerican art historical materials ranging from jade figures to carved monuments. Those materials used in the art history exercises will be drawn from the professor's own vast collection of images and selected personal specimens. The objective will be to develop an appreciation and knowledge of how to differentiate Maya from other art historical specimens.

Lab Module 2: The Maya Glyph Workshop

The Maya Glyph Workshop was developed so as to provide participants with a hands-on comprehension of the art of Mesoamerican epigraphy or glyphic writings. Originally developed by the earlier Olmec civilization of present-day Veracruz and Tabasco, Mexico, the art of logo and phonetic-based writing systems, originated prior to the advent of the Common (or Christian) Era. In this series of lab exercises, participants will be provided an overview of the logographic and phonetic structure of the language. They will then be asked to render their individual names, places of birth, and birth years in Maya phonetic and logographic scripts on parchment paper. Other aspects of the lab will include "transcription" of ancient Maya monuments, and associated calendrical dates.

Lab Module 3: The Mesoamerican Codex Workshop

Drawing on photo-quality reproductions of Mesoamerican codices, or screenfold books, from ancient Mexico and the Maya region, students will be introduced to the iconographic and logographic analyses, and historiography of said books. Students will be tasked with the objective of identifying individual political, cultural, and religious icons and depictions. Outcomes include seeking to identify and interpret cultural meanings and contemporary understandings.

Lab Module 4: Mesoamerican Gods and Deities

This lab will explore the complex pantheon of gods, deities, and supernaturals that inhabited the Mesoamerican, and Maya, worlds. Drawing on extant identifications of deity-specific icons, students will seek to understand and interpret one or more dimensions of the Maya system of cosmological belief. A prime objective will be to construct a flow chart of supernaturals and their respective celestial and terrestrial associations.

Lab Module 5: Ancient Games and Gamesmanship

Participants will be challenged to learn one of two ancient games -- mainly, Maya Pok-ta-pok, Bul, or Aztec Patolli. In each instance, you will first learn the basic rules of your selected game via online or electronic versions of said game located at those Internet links posted on the links page. Each student participant will then be challenged at the end of the term to compete for as much as ten percentage points of extra credit. Your goal is to both learn the game as well as a bit of the psychology of the culture that produced the game in question. At present, Aztec and Maya patolli is the primary game used in this exercise.
