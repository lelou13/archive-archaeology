Recommended Readings

The following readings have been posted here so as to provide you with a basic orientation to that literature that exists in the realm of Maya studies. Please bear in mind that while much of the following list is drawn from the field of archaeology, ethnohistory, and epigraphy, the reality is that Maya studies span the fields of anthropology, archaeology, ethnohistory, history, cultural ecology, demography, epigraphy, and a host of other areas of sociological, civilizational, and art historical analysis. This list will be updated and amended on an ongoing basis, but those selections noted should provide at least one basis for a beginning in Maya studies.

Selected Readings


Coe, Michael D. 1992. Breaking the Maya Code. New York: Thames and Hudson.

Coe, Michael D. 1998. The Art of the Maya Scribe. Michael D. Coe, Justin Kerr. New York: Harry N. Abrams.

Miller, Mary Ellen.  1999.  Maya Art and Architecture.  London: Thames & Hudson.

Sabloff, Jeremy A. 1990. The New Archaeology and the Ancient Maya. New York: Scientific American Library.

Schele, Linda, and David Freidel. 1990.  A Forest of Kings: The Untold Story of the Ancient Maya.  New York: William Morrow and Company, Inc.

Tedlock, Dennis (Translator).  1996. Popol Vuh: The Mayan Book of the Dawn of Life.  Touchstone Books.



*Note: See http://astro.temple.edu/~dcm/maya.htm for a useful research guide to the Classic Maya. The FAMSI Bibliografia Mesoamericana provides a comprehensive search function and database for Maya studies bibliographic materials at http://research.famsi.org/mesobib.html.
