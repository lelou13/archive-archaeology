Course Description

Explores the multicultural heritage of an early California community using primary resource documents, secondary sources, and service-based community oral history collections and narrative traditions. Using existing resources and project-based discoveries and collected works, students recover and rediscover the multicultural heritage of the early California communities of the Central Coast. Current projects include the historic photograph and documents collections of the Luck Library and Museum of San Juan Bautista.

Culture & Equity
This class has been developed to meet the Culture & Equity ULR, however, this course is currently under review for said outcome.  As such, we will address this requirement in anticipation of approval by terms end.  Of course, in order to fully address the Culture & Equity ULR, the following guidelines will need to be met by way of your final project.

The University Learning Requirement (ULR) on Culture and Equity is required of all CSUMB students to graduate. To meet this ULR, student must demonstrate a comprehension of one's individual cultural identity in relationship to other cultures and lifestyles within their contexts; and demonstrate critical awareness of relations of power as well as means for creating greater equity and social justice.

What does this mean?

This ULR combines the former Culture and Equity ULRs into a single ULR. To satisfy this learning requirement, students must demonstrate that they: 1. Comprehends the concept of culture and can apply this concept to the comparison of one’s own culture with that of other cultures;

2. Comprehends the concept of cultural identity and can apply this concept to the comparison of one’s own cultural identity with the cultural identity of people from other cultures;

3. Comprehends the concepts of power relations, equity, and social justice; and can apply these concepts to U.S. society as well as other societies;

4. Demonstrates critical awareness of the relations of power that exist between different cultural groups in the United States and in other societies;

5. Demonstrates critical awareness of how to create greater equity and social justice in the United States and in other societies.

6. Comprehends historical context of cultural discrimination, inequity and social justice.

Challenge Project Option

While a number of local and regional histories have been written about California and the Monterey Bay, such histories do not tend to be “multicultural” in the strictest sense.  By multicultural I am referring to those histories and narratives that draw upon the broadest representation of those contributions and voices that have come to constitute the multicultural core of the Monterey Bay.  For instance, back in the summer of 1995 I collected a brochure from the Monterey Chamber of Commerce that reviewed the “history” of the adobes.  After a brief note, the “history” began with a single sentence devoted to the several thousand-year history of the American Indian on the Central Coast, and then jumped to one additional sentence on the Spanish colonization of the bay.  Then, the narrative skipped past the Mexican era when most of the adobes were constructed, and then jumped headlong into the history of “famous” or “infamous” Americans who later lived in those same adobes.  I would not construe this to be a multi-culturally informed history of Monterey or its adobes.  On the other hand, most available local histories tend to be devoted to some aspect of the region’s history, or focus on a singular group.  These too tend not to be “multicultural” in the broadest sense.  As such I pose the following challenges and offer those rewards for efforts in this direction that might be appropriate.

You are cautioned however to provide sufficient evidence or documentation for your study so as to weigh the evidence both for and against your position or creation.  By taking on this project you will be allowed the option of foregoing, or not being required to complete, the Midterm and End term Reviews, and or the Final Project.  Credit for either option will depend on the extent of documentation, detail, and accuracy of those byproducts produced.  If you wish to forego both the Midterm and End term reviews, or the final project, you might consider completing more than one of the challenges noted below.  Please note that no Incomplete grades or assignments will be permitted in this instance.  In order to take on one of the following challenge options, you will need to provide a brief proposal of that which will constitute the length and breadth of your efforts to document a multicultural heritage.  The challenge options are as follows:

1. The Multicultural Timeline: Creation of a detailed “multicultural timeline” complete with dates, places, events and or contributions made by a range of ethnic and cultural groups on the Central Coast.  Though the emphasis is intended to be “multicultural,” you may choose to focus on a specific ethnic or cultural group’s contributions so long as it represents a group for which little has been done in this regard in the past (e.g., Filipino, Ohlone, Vietnamese, African American, Mexican American, Irish American, etc.).  See California in Time or Chronology of California’s Transition (California State Parks).

2. The Racialization Retrospective: Creation of a detailed “racialization” retrospective or chronology of events that serves to document issues pertinent to those outcomes expected of the Culture & Equity ULR.  The focus here should be with regard to structures and actions that served to undermine interracial harmony, equity, and social justice on the Monterey Bay.

3. The Community Futures Lab: Creation of a well documented and detailed framework for how it is that you would seek to resolve the persistent pattern of racialization, social and economic inequities, and patterns of social injustice that tend to pervade inter-ethnic and cultural relations on the California Central Coast.  Please note: You need not argue that such problems can be resolved; but rather, provide a model for how you might go about promoting solutions given the resource base and economic and historical realities of the day.

The Luck Museum Project Option

The Luck's Library and Museum Collection:  The Carl Martin Luck Library and Museum is located at the corner of Third and Monterey Streets in San Juan Bautista.  The Luck Museum is situated at the southwest edge of the park named for Carl Martin Luck, and it is within the Luck Museum that the archival collections that may provide one of the project options are currently located.
The Luck Museum collection consists of historic documents, photographs, maps, and some artifacts related to San Juan Bautista's early history.  The collection consists primarily of post-Mission era 19th century Mexican and American materials.  In addition to a large collection of mid to late 19th and early 20th century newspapers and photographs, the collection also contains historic survey records of the old buildings and adobes of San Juan Bautista.

One other potential term project is that of producing an oral history through interviews with long time San Juan Bautista, Salinas, or Monterey residents.  A number of such residents are direct descendants of the earliest Spanish, Mexican, Asian, and American pioneers to the region.

Portfolio Format

Final Portfolio: All project-related materials produced this semester, including all relevant inserts, typewritten deliverables, lab and field forms and typewritten journal entries, should be included in a final portfolio submitted at end of term. In addition, your final portfolio should include an electronic copy of all labs and field notes and related written materials. All electronic and hard copy will be used to document project efforts for class use. Your final Self Assessment of this learning experience, and an overview of your respective service learning contributions (i.e., online exhibit development and or public education programming), should be included within the final portfolio.

Portfolio Format: All term deliverables are to be assembled in a portfolio binder consisting of (a) black or white 1.0" or 1.5" vinyl-covered three-ring binder (preferably with a transparent overlay for a cover page insert), and should include (b) a clearly formatted cover page including course title and section number, the student's name, semester and date, telephone number and e-mail address, and actual date at which the portfolio was submitted (to be included as a page insert within the top cover of the portfolio binder and in addition inserted into the first page of the bound portfolio); (c) a table of contents listing all inserts and deliverables, (d) all self-assessment and instructor-based assessments, term deliverables (i.e. field and lab journal and typewritten essays and related materials), and all completed lab and field log forms and notes, and final project-related materials should be organized in the portfolio with transparent page protectors or sleeves for each deliverable or assignment included in the portfolio, (d) tabbed section dividers (color-coded or labeled), and (e) appropriate transparent sleeves for any cassette, diskette, or video recordings or electronic documents that you may plan to include in your portfolio.

Note: No other attachments (e.g. printed web pages used as supplementary materials) should be included or inserted into the final portfolio submitted for end term evaluation. In addition, all documents entered into the final portfolio are required to include your name, course section number (i.e. SBSC 220s), and a date of submission (e.g. 9-14-01 for September 14, 2001) placed in the upper right hand corner of each and every page submitted. In the lower right-hand corner of each and every page (excluding the title page), page numbers should be clearly and accurately listed.
