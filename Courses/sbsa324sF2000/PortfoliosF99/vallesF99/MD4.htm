MAJOR DELIVERABLE 4
What was life like for the indigenous peoples before The Missionaries arrived to Central California how did life change and what did it change into?
That is a question that arose during the time the research was underway.  It has become the focus of the entire research because before the coming of the Spaniards to Central California had the densest Indian population anywhere north of Mexico.  Now, it is the area, which is lastly recognized for that fact.  Central California is recognized more for the famous writer John Steinbeck than for the rich culture that has become vastly forgotten.
This report will be tied to the San Juan Bautista Mission, which lies merely 17 miles north of Salinas, and 4 miles south of U.S. Highway 101.  It was founded on June on the 24th day in 1797.  It was founded by Fr. Fermín Lasuén.  San Juan was named after John the Baptist and I chose it because it lies close by, and in addition to that because of the excavation research I have conducted there (Johnson, Paul 1979, 1967:241).

 I will discuss in a more thorough manner what the Native America’s lost to the encounter and what the goals for the encounter with the Spaniards and Missionaries.  I would like to explore what was going on economically and socially that lead Spain to impose cultural genocide of the Native peoples.
 Just as well, I will touch upon the Missionaries perspective on the “enlightening” of their arrival.  Ultimately I would like to discuss a period in time when nature and familism were the core of a society and what levels of organization existed, and how that deteriorated through the time passed.  How it came to be that the Missionaries, who came to civilize, cultivate, and spread the word of God went against what they were preaching.
 Economics and the push of religion were the main driving forces behind the colonization of the Native Americans in Alta. California, particularly Monterey.  The result of this colonization and the genocide of countless people, was a loss of a culture.  A forced acculturation was the result to the entering of Missionaries and Spanish explorers in the land the Natives inhabited.

 When Spain’s hierarchy ruled a large portion of the world, under Fernidad and Isabella, riches were the priorities.  Wherever they found abundance, they claimed the land in the name of the crown (Johnson, Paul 1967:35).

 When the Spanish arrived in California, they encountered a territory already heavily occupied.  Population estimated have generally ranged in the area of 133, 000 (Kroeber 1925) to 310, 000 (Cook 1978) for the number of Indians in California at the time of White contact.   There was already a sense of an organized society.  Where there existed more gender equality than there is in our society, a trade-based economy, and complete use of the local use of resources.  There was recognition of the geographical area by the Natives, and the vegetation or the mineral resources were used in moderation.

 One of the attractions that lured the Spanish was the gold, the port of Monterey and the rich vegetation that exists still today.  Vizcaino was the primary expeditionary who was sent to the coastal area of Monterey, which he named after the Conde de Monterey.   After the “discovery” of a land, which was occupied by Costanoan Natives, a plan to bring in Missions was created.  It was a known fact from the first contact with the Natives that they didn’t have guns or arms of steel, making them easier to conquer.  The decision to create missions was to model the success they brought in Mexico.  It would be an efficient method that had already proved to function.

 Missions killed two birds with one stone, not only did they acculturate the Natives, but they also trained them to various trades of work.  They were trained to do everything from leather work, texturing, or whatever other trade would bring money.  By doing so, they would become tax-paying citizens that would assist in making the crown stronger.

 What I have found through the research I have done is consistency.  Many times the objective of the Mission was to spread Christianity and other times it was to “civilize.”  Interestingly, the two goals went hand-in hand; to “civilize” was to teach the word of the Christian God.  One of the primary objectives of the Franciscan directed mission programs in Alta. California was the transformation of the culture and world view of the Indian converts congregated in the missions.   What they missionaries brought with them was a superiority complex to believe that they were coming to save people and with the capacity of transforming a people they didn’t know about.

  In order to understand the perspective of the Franciscans and Spaniards we must understand the pressures they were experiencing.  There was an enormous demand for the missions to develop ways to make moneys and use the Natives as resources from Spain.

 The mentality of the Franciscan was quite different from the Spanish Expeditionary.  The missionaries, regardless to their personal blemishes as humans, truly believed that they were doing well for the Natives and that they were saving them.  Father Junipero Serra, who founded the missions of Alta. California wrote, “Missions my lord, missions-that is what this country needs.   They carried out the beliefs of Christianity and considered themselves messengers from God, but the manner in which they carried out the word of God was not a peaceful one. For example, to make sure they learned the virtues of hard work, the monks eschewed labor-saving devices and deliberately taught the Indians methods that were difficult and outdated even at that time (Margolin, Malcolm 1978:162).  They administered cruel punishments to transform the Natives into “gente de razon” –the European way of life.

  The Natives had knowledge of the land that they occupied without books and lived a rather peaceful and organized life with out any kind of governmental intervention.  They named the trees and the parts of each tree, its leaves and branches, distinguishing between the various species.  They named the heavens, the sun and moon and all the stars they could identify.  They identified and named parts of the human body, and had knowledge of the circulatory system, so that their Native doctors could minister them with knowledge and ability (Costo, Rupert and Jeanette 1995:4).  Besides having an accurate recognition of astrology, anatomy, botany they had ceremonies and a nature-based religion and medicinal knowledge.

 In the Native society, the elders were the largest most influential party.  Familism was the central driving factor in everyday life for the Natives.  People weren’t forced to take responsibilities, a person did what the person did best, whether due to superior strength, desire to do that kind of work, some special ability, or traditional types of labor.  In times of stress or catastrophe, everyone did what was needed.  There wasn’t any gender roles, if anything women were more appreciated than in our very own “advanced” society, in most groups she was respected as the keeper of the culture, the bearer of the race and the protector of its existence and continuance (Costo, Rupert and Jeanette 1995:48).

 Once the Spanish Inquisition came to Monterey, and the Franciscans created a mission in San Juan Bautista, many changes took place.  Natives were physically forced to live a way unfamiliar and unnatural to them.  The main reason behind the majority of the deaths and the leading cause behind the disappearance of the population of Natives were European diseases brought by expeditions.  The most spectacular period of mortality among the American Indians occurred during the first hundred years of contact with the Europeans.   The Natives knew it was upon them, but could do nothing about it.  There seems to be a lot of resentment on the part of the Native populations in this day and age for what they went through.  Here you have an Indian tell of happier days and his people before the Europeans came,
 

There was no sickness: they had no aching bones; they had no fever; they had then no smallpox; they had then no abdominal pain; they had then no consumption; they had then no headache.  At that time the course of humanity was orderly.  The foreigners made it otherwise when they arrived here.

 In summary, I found that what occurred was an ambitious, unconscious genocide of the Native Americans who had their culture ripped from them and they were reduced to substituting animas in the work that they were physically forced to complete.  The Spaniards succeeded in their acculturation and not only did they make the Natives transform, but also they eliminated the possibility of survival for many Native American Nations.

 In conclusion, it was painful to go through this research because my of my ancestral roots and how this research told of how it is that we lost who we may have originally been.  However, I have reached the realization that the Franciscans, who came with the best intentions, they came to spread the word of God and “help” the Indians.  Although I feel resentful of their arrival, as with the Spaniards, there is nothing to be done.  The only thing we could do is to try to retain the culture we have and not let go.  The missionaries thought they were doing the Indians a favor by introducing them to the agricultural methods, clothing, food, traditions, and a new language among other things.

 Bringing things to a more geographically recognized mission, we have San Juan Bautista as introduced earlier.

Beginning with the year it was built, 1797, I will take you through its stages.  It is interesting to note where the priority in the building process went.

1779 Buildings constructed including a chapel, quarters for missionaries, soldiers’ barracks, kitchen, granary and dormitory for single girls and women.
1798 A large granary built.
1799-1806 Main quadrangle of the mission built.

1803-12 A new large church, the present structure, built.

 It is also interesting to note that San Juan had the largest estimated number of fugitives by the year 1832, compared to the other missions (Castillo, Edward and Jackson, Robert H.1995:162).  Which again brings us to question the treatment of people who have to result to crime.  As one San Juan Bautista father mentions, they didn’t perceive Indians to be very intelligent, which could have resulted in treating them badly.  Father Engelhardt believed the summary of his work was as conducting “huge training schools, which transformed indolent and ignorant savages into Christians and self-supporting members of civil society…”

 Many times the two clashed, the Indian and the missionary, as in a story told by a local Salinan Indian about a medicine man and a missionary.
Long ago there was an old shaman who had a reputation as a rainmaker who could make rain whenever he wished.  One year there was a long drought and the Padre of the Mission said, “we will test his powers.”  He gave orders that the old man should be caught and brought before him.  Then he said to him, “If you do not make it rain so that it will fill these barrels I will have you ties and whipped.”

 It is good,” replied the shaman, “I will try.”  Then he sang.  Soon the sky became overcast and with clouds and it thundered.  Then came the rain furiously; it did not delay long.  The barrels which the Padre had placed were filled quickly.  Then he told the man to stop the rain.  And it stopped.  “We do not wish anymore,” he said.  “No, there is enough already!”—Pedro Encinales,Salinan

 Here is yet another example of the little faith, or inferiority the missionaries felt towards the Indians.  It will be a long time, with endless tests before all people are given credit and acknowledgments for their accomplishments.
