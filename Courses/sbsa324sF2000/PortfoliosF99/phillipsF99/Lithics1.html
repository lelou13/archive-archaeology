The two data sets I have represented in bar graph form are B13 and J11. Each column represents a batch of debitage collected that falls into size increments in centimeters, as shown with the x-axis of the chart beginning with <1 centimeters up to 7-9 centimeters. The y-axis shows increments of 5 units representing both grams (weight) and centimeters (size). These two charts show the relationship between the size of the pieces and their different type of material (jasper) which has a different weight than obsidian. The weight of the jasper is shown in blue and labeled weight – A in the key. Also in the chart for B13, there is a green bar that goes off the chart. This was a very heavy piece found in the 7-9 cm category. I decided not to make the chart big enough to show the exact size visually since its ration to the other weights is obviously of significant difference.
With further analysis, this data could be used to infer possible tool use. By looking at the size and weight of the debitage and where it landed, many theories can be drawn, such as where the flintknapper was sitting and what he was possibly creating. A lot of tiny flakes in one area would indicate that the flintknapper possibly finished a tool nearby since he (or she) would be doing finishing touches in finer detail evidenced by small flakes. This would also mean that the tool would not be present since the flintknapper would have taken it away to use it. A large piece found could indicate that the flintknapper found the shape or other quality of the stone undesirable or was interrupted from his flintknapping before he could finish the piece.
