Technical terminology
Stones are created by geological forces including trees absorbing minerals-petrification, opalization
Fracturing properties important-hitting obsidian or chert sends a shock wave through the stone and a blade peels away from the stone
Methods of breaking stone:
Hammerstone: hardhammer percussion; using stone against stone; you can’t control the direction or force; both materials are likely to give
Soft hammer percussion: using an antler (solidified hair) you are better able to direct the angle of force due to the threads from the hair
Indirect percussion: a flaking tool (usually an antler) is hit with another tool
Pressure flaking: instead of striking the stone, you are peeling away material with an antler or small stone
Flakes: pieces of stone removed to create a tool
Flake ridge: seam where two flakes have been knocked away
Bulb of percussion: bulbous area from the shock wave follows the seam of the stone; these do not occur naturally; creates a wave pattern throughout the stone (compression rings)
Distal end: (tip) pointed end
Proximal end: (base) part attached to the arrow
All cultures have arrowheads; like the Swiss army knife; used for many functions
Stages:
Point blank-can be used as a scraper
Point preform-unfinished tools
Discarded point – after sharpening and resharpening, the point gets too small to use
Cornernotch – arrowhead
Dartheads are usually broader
Biface – two faces elaborately flaked
Blade tools – main tool of the Americas, widespread
Neckwidth – distance from notch to notch
