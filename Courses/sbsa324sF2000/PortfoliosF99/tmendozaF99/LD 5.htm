Lab Deliverable 5:  Readings Review

1. In the quest for the "Lost Spanish Mission" David Hurst Thomas and his crew began by random sampling, measuring series of the area by walking a number of steps and 100 meters wide to find areas that they would dig. This method did take his crew to areas hard to reach, and often leading to nothing. Thomas and his crew then evaluated the whole Catherines Island, studying unrecorded areas, searching for lost missions such as Santa Catalina de Guale. Investigating each site in meter square test units, the crew ended up excavating more than 400 such test pits. With the search looking a little dim, Thomas and his crew began to look towards other methods. The next methods that Thomas and his crew tried was one, a method they learned from Kathleen Deagan, using a gasoline-powered posthold digger, digging hundreds of round holes. With the results, a series of simple dog-density maps were plotted. This method allowed the crew to focus further on a single 100 by 100 meter square in the overall sampling grid for the St. Catherines Island. Another method the crew used was nondestructive remote sensing. I believe that the big disadvantage Thomas and his crew had in this whole project was not knowing where to start. The advantages that were on their side was past excavations that could assist them in trying to figure out where to start and how to start, such as the help they received from Deagan. I think the dig was employed succesfully with the circumstances. The excavation illustrates the "maturation in American archaeology" showing that with scientific techniques, an excavation have some kind of result and the methods grow more and more advanced. Such as Remote sensing, it allowed data to be found before anyone had to actually dive in and start digging.  In the long run, this will add to the advancement of archaeology field work.
 

2. During an archaeological search, many archaeologist are digging in blind, not really knowing what they will find and if they will find anything to support their research. As a result, archaeologists have modified their old ways of simply blasting away at their sites often leaving a big mess, to the modern ways of conducting "Test Pits" which quotes David Hurst Thomas, "tend to be quick and dirty."  With this method, archaeologist then dig "metrically" typically 1 meter square. maintaining a three-dimensional control with an x,y, and z axis. Sidewalls are created to keep the area straight and perpendicular, keeping the area clean and a specific area where findings can be accurately marked. This new way of modern Archaeological digging is also due to the new ethical ways that digging should be less and to save more of the archaeological remains for the future, believing that archaeological grandchildren will have invented and possess far greater methods, theories, and technology for the archaeological world. With professor Mendoza's field work at the Old Mission San Juan Bautista, he also digs in squares, then dividing the big square in four little equal squares making his north, south, west, east areas. The only difference with Thomas and professor Mendoza's work is perhaps the way he has marked each dig site, where Thomas uses axis Mendoza uses N,S,W. From my own experience at the site, it's profoundly a better way to dig at an area where you can specifically mark where you have found data.
