The class topic for today's journal was about Urban Material Culture, which is the
study of Social - standing, political, religious, and ideology of urban cultures. Dr. Mendoza
went into Chicano/Latina sub-cultures in Arizona, to study how these groups used graffiti,
low- riders, and murals to depict their cultures. Dr. Mendoza documented (as an anthropologist)
why the sub-cultures used different symbols to represent their culture and beliefs. For example,
Chicano/Latina communities in the Southwest and in California started to make cars into the low-
riders in the early fifties, to represent the different car clubs or sub-cultures. To be a member of
the car clubs, the person had to be drug-free, clean car, in good standing within the community,
show up to all the meetings. Main stream America used to believe that all low-riders were gang
and on drugs. The Chicano sub-groups would use unique techniques on their low-riders to separate
their car club from other clubs. The forms of expression they used were murals depicting different
types of religious scenes, or symbols of women. The different members who belong to the car club
name-      plates of the low-riders have various names and colors. Dr. Mendoza studied the murals
painted by the Chicana/Latina sub-culture and documented the low-riders because he thought this
type of urban material culture would be lost. Dr. Mendoza went into the Latina communities as an
anthropologist to study and document what life was like within these sub-cultures. Murals have been
used to depict about the way of life since the beginning of time. The murals are used within the sub-
cultures for the purpose of reading (a visual image) for someone who couldn't read. Murals signified
what the community a particular community valued. Rival groups may vandalize many of the murals.
They would use spray paint to vandalize the murals. The young kids left alone all day resort to these
sub-cultures where they look for protection, drugs, and social identification.

     Bruce Bradley's video, "Flintknapping," shows the process of how to make obsidian into a
     variety of stone tools. The process was tedious and has to be done in a precise manner,
     or the obsidian will fracture. Understanding how plate stone tools have been used for
     centuries, will help archaeologists better understand "human technologies." Bradley
     shows how a large piece of flake material can be used for large tools used for hunting
     bison. The stone tool can be flaked down to a 90' degree angle to make a serrated edge.
     stone tool that can be used for cutting pieces of meat. A flake blade tool can be used to
    scrape down deer, antelope, and bison hides into suede like material. Obsidian can also
     be used for making spear points. This process is critical and tedious. Scrappers, and
     stones, different shapes of antlers are used to shape the material into the point shape.
     Smaller flakes that brake away during the shaping process, can be used to make other
     stone tools. Different shaped antlers are used to make the obsidian thinner and flatter,
     which shapes the point. Finally, a spatula tool is used to make the notches in each side of
     the spear point.

     The major myth of the Mound Builders was that they were from North America. They were
     thought to be nomads, Vikings who were lost people passing through North America on
     They're way to Central Mexico. Europeans refused to believe these massive mounds could
     be built by Native American cultures. They thought they were too savage and unskilled to
     build the mounds. During the early excavations, the researchers found evidence from
     skulls, that confirmed that two ancient Native American cultures did create these massive
     mounds. Thomas who was sent by the government, to finally resolve these myths,
     discovered two cultures. These "Mound Builders" were called the Hopewell people and
     the Mississippian people. Today, Salvage excavation is used by archaeologist to
     research the ancient artifacts and to better understand these two cultures better. However,
     on the other hand, Salvage excavation invades the ancient burial grounds. This may be
     why Native Americans believe white people discrete and loot their ancient burial sites.
     Native Americans believe they have skills to research these ancient "Mound Builders" in a
     way that does not disturb these sites. However, archaeologists believe it is important to
     excavate these sites in St. Louis to better understand the world around us. Why and where
     did man migrate from? To understand the "hunter/gather" and their way of life. Also, to
     preserve these "mounds" before modern civilization demolishes them forever. Without
     this type of research, these answers will be lost...
