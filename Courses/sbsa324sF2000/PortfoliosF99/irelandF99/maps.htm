Archaeology Laboratory Experiment: Map Sampling


It is through the use of maps that an archaeologist locates her site, but once the site has been reached, there could be anywhere from a few meters to a few hectares worth of land to survey. Obviously, this presents a problem, in terms of both time and money, since an archaeologist would probably not have unlimited time to neither discover all the artifacts at a particular location, nor would she have the funding for such an endeavor. Therefore, an archaeologist must use techniques to limit his data while at the same time allow enough room for exploration to discover artifacts over a broad scale. Unfortunately, this produces another quandary, by virtue of the fact that by limiting data, an archaeologist limits her ability to produce an accurate picture of the people she is studying. She must sample the map, and do so in a way that she feels, guesses or theorizes would provide the most data during the time allotted.

A few sampling strategies tried in the class were random sampling, transecting, prediction sampling and stratified sampling. Random sampling is picking a few squares from the map totally at random. Obviously, this strategy offers the least promise for accuracy, since the archaeologist is doing little but blundering her way around the site in hopes of finding something. Transecting a site is picking a series of cells in line on the map grid and walking along them, looking for anything that might seem to be of an artificial nature. This offers quite a bit more promise for accuracy, especially if the transect is chosen carefully. Prediction sampling is the selecting of map cells according to how people may have lived on the land; i.e., near water or food sites, on top of hills, etc. This offers a good chance of accuracy as well, but only if the people conform to modern conceptions and standards. Finally, a stratified sample is taken by filtering out data already gathered from a specific site and selecting map-squares that have not been previously surveyed in order to build on a theory, or find new evidence.

Transecting:

Prediction Sampling:

By first taking into account the fact that Xochicalco was a settlement during the Epiclassic period, a time of constant warring, I selected squares that I believed would show evidence of possible attack. I looked at the map as an attacking warlord would and selected squares that would be in the main avenues of assault (without repeating squares already picked randomly) They are as follows: A8, A12, J11, K10, L7, L11, M6, O11, U2 and V5. Both U2 and V5 had evidence, but none of the rest had anything. 20% success rate

Stratified Sample:

After making all of these decisions, I selected squares I believed were without "noise", so to speak. The following squares seemed to hold the most promise for dig sites, when taking into account the previous evidence:

A9: It covers a large portion of settlement XF-215, but also has a flat area just to the east of it, wide open to attack.

J12: This covers a good section of the structure on the hill. If invaders came in from the northeast, the grade of the hill to the immediate northwest would provide an easy ascent for attacking armies.

K9: It covers a courtyard-like section of another structure, perhaps providing an insight into the city dwellers' lifestyle, as well as providing information on whether this, the largest structure in the area, was invaded.

L9: A continuation of the structure from square K9, this square also covers an area that an invading army might have marched down after having attacked the structure at J12. Being jjust to the west of a land-bridge with ravines on either side of it, this area was in the path of such an attack.

M7: Another structure atop this hill-complex. It would provide further clues into the city-dwellers, and is also the only waypoint between the large structure and the only shallow grade down to the river running northwest of the hill-complex. Invading armies surely would have come through here.

O10: This covers the area of residence, XT-2. This may have been bypassed by armies, due to its shelter in the lee of the main hill. Investigation may prove otherwise, or may show how the people lived before attack.

Unfortunately, 010 was the only site that showed any evidence. 10% success rating.

Considering the selections in the stratified sample, the failure of finding much evidence goes to show I should have done more research into the area and learned what had been discovered previously. Transecting turned out to be my best bet--I would have had a much better chance of success had I used that particular sampling strategy.
