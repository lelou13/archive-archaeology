Notes on "Myths and Moundbuilders"

Whites have been fascinated by the large earth structures—mounds, ditches, dikes, etc.—of North America, and considered them not as proof that the Indians were capable of organized society, but instead considered them to be proof of a "lost white civilization". *snicker*

The mounds have been attributed to the Vikings, the Welsh, the Lost Tribes of Israel, or even a stop made by the people on their way to build the civilizations of Mesoamerica.

Morton’s use of craniometry showed that the moundbuilders’ skulls were almost exactly the same as those of modern Indians, so he fabricated a story claiming that there were two races—a lost white race which had been destroyed by the "barbarous" Indians. This fabrication was used as justification by the white settlers to push the Indians off their homelands. The land had originally belonged to the whites, hadn’t it? *snort*

A survey performed by Squire and Davis resulted in the claim that most mounds were defensive ramparts. Artifacts were looted from mounds while they were being ploughed under by expansionist farmers. Early French and Spanish explorers described natives as living in an agricultural society with leaders living on mounds of earth. The evidence could be construed as backing up the Squire/Davis survey, but the "defensive ramparts" theory was instead used to back up the theory that the lost white civilization was wiped out by the "barbarous" Indians.

Faulk of Washington D.C. was allotted $5,000 by the U.S. Congress to settle the question of the moundbuilders once and for all. Thomas believed that the Indians did indeed construct the mounds and was well aware that the belief in "lost white civilizations" was an addictive, romantic fantasy. He proved connectgions between the mounds and the Indians. Some of the largest mounds were destroyed in the building of St. Louis.

Two cultures did indeed exist, but none of them were white, and they didn’t exist at the same time. They were the Hopewell (B.C. 100 – A.D. 100 or 200), who built conical mounds; and the Mississippian (A.D. 800 – A.D. 1200), who built flat-topped, pyramidal mounds for their chiefs and temples.

An interstate will be built across sites in the way, so modern archaeologists need to excavate quickly in order to salvage as much as possible before the sites are destroyed. This process is called "salvage archaeology". Artifacts were found that suggested a vast trading network. The odyssey crew built a mound to find out exactly what sort of societal impact that sort of construction would have had. This process is called "experimental archaeology".

In order to further understand this, villages were excavetd and their leavings examined. They worked copper, they constructed fishhooks & lines and hunted & gathered also to understand how the Indians may have done it. Sumpweed seeds were larger during the Hopewell period, leading to the theory that the Hopewell Indians cultivated this plant through the selection and cultivation of this particular plant. The archaeologists estimated that there were approximately 50 people per square mile of river, moer people than exist today with at least the same agricultural productivity as today’s. Aslo, moundbuilding took relatively little effort. An average-sized mound could be built in an afternoon, with about 40 people. The introduction of maize (corn) changed the Hopewell culture. They became the Mississippians. The people moved onto hills and populations increased, while general health decreased as did artifacts. Larger mounds were created—Monk’s Mound, the larges man-mae structure north of Mexico – a civilization of about 50,000 people ruled by a chiefly elite. Civilization & nobility arose as a result of the new maize-based civilization. Due to crop failures brought about by overfarming and overpopulation, the civilization entered a decline and began to collapse which the Spanish & French explorers accelerated. Racist theories then began to arise. Scientists and archaeologists need explanations, so times and terminologies change, sometimes with more accuracy, sometimes just to satisfy social theories and traditions. Thus arise the creation of myths and legends.
