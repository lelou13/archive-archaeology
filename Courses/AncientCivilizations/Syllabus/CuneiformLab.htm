The Cuneiform Lab

The Cuneiform Lab was developed so as to provide participants with a hands-on comprehension of the art of Mesopotamian cuneiform writing. Originally developed by the Sumerian civilization of present-day Iraq, the art of cuneiform writing, which requires the use of a reed, bone, or wood stylus and a clay tablet, originated at circa 3200 BCE. In this lab, participants are asked to render their name, place of birth, and birth year in cuneiform script on a clay tablet. This page includes examples of the results of the Spring 2005 Cuneiform Lab exercise.

The cuneiform tablet at right makes use both early pictographic elements and wedge and edge cuneiform impressions. Created by Nelia Barajas in March 2005.



A spoke shave, wedge, hammer, and electrical sander were used to craft the bamboo stylus elements used.


The Cuneiform Lab was first introduced into the SBS 332 World Prehistory course on Tuesday, March 1st of 2005. On that date, students tried their hand at the production of Mesopotamian cuneiform using both a "reed" or bamboo stylus and clay tablets. Prior to the production of said cuneiform tablets, students were first introduced to the art of cuneiform via electronic documents pertaining to LD5: Cuneiform Writing and Mesopotamian Mathematics, as well as a Cuneiform Links page in PDF format. Professor Mendoza made use of a bamboo pole in order to craft the cuneiform stylus used in this experiment. Tools used to craft stylus elements are shown at left.

 
In order to recreate the Mesopotamian cuneiform writing, a variety of Internet resources were used in this experimental archaeology lab. Discussions on cuneiform writing are available from a variety of resources, and cuneiform "alphabets" are available at a number of sites. In this instance, students were asked to generate cuneiform versions of their respective names, places of birth, year of birth, and related information in cuneiform script. The results were initially rendered on paper, and then in a subsequent lab transferred to clay tablets using a bamboo stylus crafted by Professor Mendoza.


Online resources pertaining to cuneform writing were used to model the available "alphabet" used.

Note positioning of bamboo stylus by Andrew Sturm.

This experiment in cuneiform writing soon determined that several differing techniques for the use of the stylus would produce markedly differing results. It was soon discovered that the proper formation of the cuneiform "letter" was dependent on (a) how the stylus was held, (b) the amount of pressure exerted on the clay tablet, (c) the plasticity of the clay, and (d) the perpendicular positioning of the stylus for the creation of the wedge shaped elements that so characterize cuneiform writing.
