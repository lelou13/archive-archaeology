Lab Notes 1: Sampling
Introduction: Research Designs, Sampling Strategies, and Site Survey.  Or, how archaeologists go about doing what they do and where.  The following terms pertain to the sampling strategies that archaeologists use to identify sites and their locations.  These terms are specifically relevant to Lab Deliverable 1 (Archaeological Sampling) and are drawn from Hester, Shafer and Feder (1997).

1.    Research Design: (a) Formulation, (b) Implementation, (c) Data Acquisition, (d) Data Processing, (e) Data Analysis, (f) Interpretation of Data, and (g) Dissemination of Results.

2.    Sampling Methods: (a) Nonprobabilistic Sampling (reconnaissance), (b) Probabilistic Sampling (samples collected are related in mathematical terms to the population sampled) -- i. cluster sampling ("more than one individual in the population being sampled is taken at each randomized position," p. 27), ii. nested sampling (sampling areas are selected, then samples within those areas are selected), iii. simple random sampling (total accessibility of sampled population or data universe), iv. systematic random sampling (provides uniform coverage of an area after first unit is selected by random procedure), v. systematic unaligned sampling (sample population divided into large blocks, and then smaller blocks are chose from within the larger blocks at random), vi. stratified random sampling (a means of minimizing variability within sampling strata by stratifying the population into classes, by for instance, environmental zones and natural barriers)..

    -- sample units (natural zones or arbitrary units such as grid squares, quadrats, or transects)
    -- target population (collection of all possible sample units or elements in the sampling universe)
    -- sampled population (collection of elements from which the random sample is actually drawn)

3.    Sampling: (a) sample fraction versus sample size (sample fraction is the percentage of the sampled population observed or tested; whereas sample size is the number of observations actually made), (b) sample units (size and number determined by real world considerations), (c) surface collecting, (d) excavation sampling, (e) sampling realities.

4.    Site Survey: (a) Goals, (b) Survey Phases, (c) Basic Considerations, (d) Factors in Survey Design (visibility, obtrusiveness, accessibility), (e) Background Research (literature review, local informants, history and ethnohistory, environmental variables), (f) Field Methods (pedestrian survey, subsurface survey; comparative methods, test-pitting and effectiveness), (g) Chemical survey, (h) Land-Based Remote Sensing (instrument anomaly survey; passive procedures, active procedures), and (i) Remote Sensing by Aerial Survey.

5.    Decision-Making for Sampling:

    a.    What kind of archaeological data is to be collected?
    b.    What sampling approach best fits the goals of the research design chosen?
    c.    If cluster sampling is used, then the size and shape of the sampling unit must be selected.
    d.    Type of cluster sampling appropriate to the project -- e.g. simple or nested -- must be chosen.
    e.    Sampling strategy -- either probabilistic or nonprobabilistic -- must be chosen.
    f.    If probabilistic, then the question of how one is to control for bias must be resolved.
    g.   What sampling fraction will be selected (i.e., what percentage of the sampling universe will actually be sampled?).
