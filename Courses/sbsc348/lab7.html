Lab Deliverable 7

Maya Calendar Dates and their Interpretation


Description: Using the attached illustrations of Maya monuments with Long Count inscriptions, please approximate the dating of each monument by way of the simple calculation of the passage of days from the date of August 13, 3114 B.C. I am looking only for an approximation to within a year or so of the actual date.


1. First, you will need to break into your previously assigned glyph groups. Having come to gether within your specific group, you should again delegate tasks as per the following tasks: (a) identification of glyphs, (b) interpretion of position and meaning, (c) calculation of the specific number of days represented, (d) calculation of the total number of days elapsed since August 13, 3114 B.C.

2. You will need to begin interpretations only on those monuments and those portions of monuments wherein there exists the Initial Series Glyph (ISG) which consists of an oval-shaped cartouche turned on end (like a cushion) within which are located two vertical box-like lines and the image of an animal head out of the top of which sprouts a serpent or serpentine-like horizontal bar.

3. Remember that you will generally be working from the Initial Series Glyph downward from left to right (column to row), left to right and so forth.

4. Bear in mind that (a) a shell symbol [ ] is the equivalent of the number zero, (b) a dot is equivalent to the number one, and (c) a bar is the equivalent of the number five. Similarly, anytime that you see a human or animal head with the likeness of a fleshless human mandible, the mandible is equivalent to the number ten.

5. The periods that you will need to interpret are as follows:

1 baktun = 144,000 days or 20 katuns (Maya Great Cycle @ 394.24 years)

1 katun = 7,200 days or 20 tuns (Maya generation or cycle @ 19.71 years)

1 tun = 360 days or 18 uinals (Maya year)

1 uinal = 20 days or 20 kins (Maya month)

1 kin = 1 day (Maya solar day)

6. Upon completing with the calculation of the Long Count date, please confirm the date with your professor's Maya calendar calculation program. Then calculate and compare your results with the actual day and year date provided by the computer generated program. The group that comes closest to the actual date on their respective monument wins the day's contest.
