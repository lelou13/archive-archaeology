The Mystery in History
An Interactive Learning Experience in a California Mission
Draft of September 22nd, 1998
Target Audience:

1.    High School Students enrolled in social studies classes
2.    Second language users and learners: Spanish and English
3.    Wide variety of reading levels from fully functional, critical readers to non-readers
4.    Those who generally find history and social science irrelevant to their lives

Goal:

1.    Create learning scenarios which engage students in structured social science research and which demonstrate relevance to concrete learning outcomes and personal experience.

2.    Develop multimedia that engages the use of digitized imagery, video, and related resources created by way of the Alta California Mission Research Project at San Juan Bautista.

3.    Demonstrate individual and group proficiency with the use and development of multimedia learning materials from archaeological and historical archaeological contexts.

Questions:

1.    What effect did the missions have on post-Mission era architectural styles (e.g. modern California architecture more generally)?

2.    How and why did the architecture and layout of Mission San Juan change over time?

3.    How is it possible that an entire community came to forget the prior existence of the Old Mission's original quadrangle?

4.    Is it possible that the failure of the Old Mission's priests to document the devastation of the quadrangle led to the loss of the quadrangle from community memory?

5.    Was there something that prevented the priest from keeping records?

6.    Have we not looked in all the right places?

7.    How did the missions affect the cultural development of the area?

8.    Why would you take a tour of the mission?

9.    What natural resources and environmental conditions drew people to the area?

10.    Spanish agricultural potential, established trade routes?

11.    Cultural conflict?

12.    What factors lead to cultural conflicts?: a. Native Americans/Spanish
b. Spanish/Americans 13.    What were the underlying factors related to Hispanic/Mexican migration?: a. Social and historical underpinnings
b. Resource related underpinnings
c. Economic and political factors which sped the colonization
d. Spiritual factors the mechanism by which it happen 14.    What methods do professional practitioners use to answer these questions: a. History
b. Archaeology
c. Art Historical
d. Cultural Geography
e. Historical Archaeology
f. Visual Anthropology
g. Museum Studies: i. How do you preserve and interpret the past?
ii. What the relationship between man and the environment?
iii. How has the role of the mission changed through time?
iv. Where did the porcelains – recovered archaeologically -- originate?
v. What do the artifacts have to say about the relative affluence of the Old Mission?
vi. How did trade affect the mission through the course of time? Treatment: Students visit the mission (by actual or virtual tour). Students interview appropriate historical figures, examine artifacts.

1. Oregon Trail type simulation, Carmen San Diego, Myst a. Case Studies (stories): Thomas Doak and the anchor. How would you prove that this was his anchor? Tombstone and anchor in cabinet. Scenarios:

1.    Virtual Mission Tour: a.    Students begin by asking the archaeologists: Why are you digging in the Old Mission courtyard?
b.    Archaeologists respond with answer beginning with an overview of the primary goals of archaeology, then move to the hypothetical existence of a former convento wing in the area – the Lost Convento!
c.    Local historian intervenes and argues that there never was a quadrangle at said location.
d.    Archaeologist, and project Art Historian, question the assumptions of the Historian: They contend that just because no documentary evidence exists to demonstrate the existence of the quadrangle does not mean that no quadrangle once existed at that location!
e.    Historian further argues that no documentary evidence – particularly that in the form of maps or architectural plans -- exists to indicate that the mission was built on a quadrangle layout, and therefore, it's not possible that a quadrangle existed at said location.
f.    A university Historian arrives on the scene and in turn questions the local historians handle on the information by saying: Not even the local histories provide sufficient information so as to say for sure whether or not a quadrangle existed at the Old Mission at any particular point in time.
g.    Has a lot to do with trade, resources, social interaction and strategic and defensive needs of the Old Mission community of San Juan.
h.    The Project Photo Historian counters: Though we have some indications of a quadrangle, these center mostly on only early sketches of a perimeter tower at the southern corner of what might have constituted the quadrangle.
i.    Students ask about the age or dates ascribed to the "Tower": We keep hearing from our teacher about an Old Guard Tower, is that what you're talking about, and if so, how old and how large was the tower? 2. Are there tunnels under these streets of San Juan Bautista?
