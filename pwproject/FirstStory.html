Initial Storyboard Proposal

San Juan Project
Loosely Based on the Storyboard by Nick Carlquist
Draft 1: 9-22-98

Audience Considerations

In order to determine the proper scope of this learning module, several questions must first be considered.

  • First and foremost, what group is being targeted as our primary audience?
  • Will this class, or a group of grade-school aged children, be undertaking the exercises in question?
  • How much knowledge of San Juan and archaeology will the students possess prior to undertaking the exercises?
  • For the purposes of creating the learning module in question, we will assume that the exercise is being completed by a group of college peers with a very limited prior knowledge of the mission and its surroundings.
Geography Module

Question 1:    Why was the mission built at its current location? Exercise 1:    Students will be provided with maps of San Juan specifically, and of California more generally.  Using the maps in question, the students should be able to evaluate the mission's surroundings and determine the nature of resources and existing cultural features that might have contributed to the original settlement of San Juan Bautista.

Initial observations should make clear that San Juan Mission was constructed half way between the two closest missions and served as an early waystation for travellers.  In addition, San Juan’s location near the ocean, and its place at the foot of the Gabilan Range and El Camino Viejo and Real should be noted.

A virtual tour (mapped images) should be undertaken via which the students can explore the surrounding area and see the strategic advantages of the site.  The needs of the occupants, the identity and culture of the occupants, defensive consideragion, natural foods, water sources, and agricultural demands should be considered as well.

Excavation Module

Question 2:    Identify and Describe the primary techniques and methods employed in the excavation of Old Mission San Juan Bautista. Exercise 1:    Students conduct a virtual excavation of an area in the mission and examine and interpret their findings.  They may use archaeology techniques drawn from the Patterson text. Question 3: How does archaeology engage the scientific method, particularly as this relates to the analysis of the archaeological findings and historical materials from the site of San Juan Bautista? Exercise 1: From the materials recovered in the previous exercise, students should be able to form some hypotheses as to the type of materials the inhabitants used for everyday life.  By reconstructing the artifacts, a sense of the patterns and designs, painting techniques and structure should be interpreted and described in narrative form.

Exercise 2: Younger students might reconstruct a broken dish or plate by way of piecing together shards or ceramic fragments located in a virtual archaeology inventory collection.  Students will identify like items, dates, and proceed toward a reconstruction of the artifact so as to understand the role of their reconstruction in the interpretation of a previously broken or incomplete "artifact."

Cartography Module

Question 4: Why was the Old Mission of San Juan Bautista located at its existing location?  What factors might have gone into the decision to locate a mission at this spot in California? Exercise 1:    Students will examine existing maps of the mission and its architectural features – both existing and archaeological.  This would be beneficial to their appreciation of the scope of construction activity undertaken in the past and of the mission and its architectural "style." Exercise 2:    Students will undertake a project that requires the planning and design for a hypothetical mission to be built in a new area so as to see what it was like to be a missionary and mission builder.  They should record their reasons and considerations for the site layout and location.

So as to allow students to work from an existing base of knowledge, the location of the mission might best be located in a community or region best known to the student or students (e.g., the student’s hometown). Exercise 3:    Students will engage in analysis of the site and its resources, both natural and cultural.  Students will identify all resources necessary for initiating and maintaining the lifestyle that emerged with the Hispanicization of the Mutsune Indian populace of the region. Exercise 4:    Students will calculate the total volume and types of materials necessary for the construction of the original mission quadrangle and out-buildings of the mission community.
