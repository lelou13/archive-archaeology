What's Expected of You

1. Collaboration

The success of ProSeminar/SBSC 100 depends a great deal upon your contribution. Much of what we do will require effective collaboration among all of us. Thus, you need to approach this learning experience in the spirit of collaboration, with an openness to negotiation and compromise, a willingness to give to and receive constructive criticism from your colleagues and the instructor.

2. Attendance

Effective collaboration in the context of this learning experience requires your physical presence at each seminar and lab session. We recognize that occasionally emergencies may force you to miss a session. Whenever possible, you should inform the instructor in advance of missing a session. You are responsible for work done and content covered in each session you miss.

3. Responsibilities to Each Other

You will be responsible for individually tutoring, mentoring, teaching and learning from two other students enrolled in this section. During the first week of the semester we will determine the process for choosing whom you will work with for the first seven weeks. In week seven we will choose new partners. In addition to providing helpful responses to their work in the ProSeminar, you will be responsible for helping them to catch up in the event they miss a seminar or lab.

4. Readings

There is one required reading text for this ProSeminar. Rereading America: Cultural Contexts for Critical Thinking and Writing is divided into seven sections, each of which addresses an important "myth" of American society and culture. We will generally follow the outline of this reader, unless we as a group negotiate an alternative order. The editors of this reader have provided alternate groupings for these readings which we may wish to consider. In order to complete all of the readings in this text you will need to average approximately 55 pages per week. The standard for social science and humanities courses is generally 100-125 pages per week. If you experience difficulties keeping up with the reading it may indicate that you need to work on improving your reading skills. You should speak to your instructor about this or seek assistance from the Academic Skills Achievement Program (ASAP).

You should complete the readings by the date for which they are listed on the course calendar unless, as a group, we have decided to do something else.

You or the instructor may suggest alternatives to any of the assigned readings. We will decide as a group whether to substitute a particular reading, with the stipulation that the alternative must deal generally with the same topic and be of approximately the same length.

In addition to the readings in the text, you will read a great deal of each others' writing. Most of this will occur in lab or seminar. But you will be expected to read on your own the major essays written by your collaborative partners.

5. Writing

Your writing will generally be closely linked to your reading. Much of it will be informal. The precise nature of the informal writing will depend upon what seems to work best for you. Informal writing exercises may include:

  • Prereading exercises in which you write briefly about a topic before reading an essay on it;
  • Personal responses to a particular reading;
  • Personal responses to our group discussions of readings;
  • Answering discussion questions from Rereading America;
  • Reporting an event you witnessed or were a part of;
  • Personal statement about, or response to, an issue on campus or in the larger society;
  • Responses to, and suggestions for improving, essays written by your peers.

The formal writing sometimes will be in the style of analytical and expository essays that characterize writing in the social and behavioral sciences. Others, such as your autobiography, may be in a more narrative or story-telling style. These will include:

  • Autobiography (to become an introduction to your Individual Learning Plan);
  • Mini-Web & Journal Research Essay on a topic in the social and behavioral sciences (Collaborative);
  • Census Research Essay on the Monterey Bay Region (Collaborative);
  • Report on Field Trip to Monterey County Historical Society (collaborative);
  • Position Essay on a topic related to a project which the group will decide upon by Monday, September 8;
  • Individual Learning Plan;
  • Evaluations of Collaboration Skills.
  • Synthesis Essay bringing together, connecting and synthesizing in an analytical essay the content and conclusions of your Autobiography, Position Essay, Field Trip Report, Mini-Web & Journal Research Essay, and Census Research;

6. Oral Communication

Informal:

  • You will be expected to participate in organized group discussions in seminars and labs. We will focus both on the readings and on topics which we as a group choose. This will include conversations we will have in the first two weeks to decide on a group service project related to the University-wide ProSeminar theme of "Building a Multicultural Campus Community."

Formal:

  • You will make a formal presentation to the group which reports either on a specific aspect of the group service project (with the goal of persuading your audience that the project either was or was not valuable to you or those for whom the service was rendered), or presents an argument for the conclusions in your Synthesis Essay. The presentation will be 8-10 minutes.

7. Portfolio

Formal written work for ProSeminar/SBSC 100 may be submitted in first draft by e-mail to the instructor. Final versions, however, must be in hardcopy and submitted in a portfolio. Your informal writings should be included in the portfolio in their original forms.
