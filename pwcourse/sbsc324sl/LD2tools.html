Archaeology: From Map to Museum
Lab Deliverable 2: Stone Tool Analysis
Introduction: The production of stone tools is an activity that first appeared at the dawn of human culture.  Because of the virtually indestructable nature of the materials used -- mainly flint, chert, and obsidian -- the residues of human stone tool production and use can be preserved for literally millions of years.  As such, we have stone tools and related evidence from ancient East Africa of the first human or hominid tool makers of some 2.5 million years ago.

Directions: This Lab Deliverable will entail two meetings during which time you will be (a) introduced to the means by which stone tools were crafted by ancient societies (Session 1: via video and a hands-on demonstration by your instructor), and (b) via a hands-on stone tool experiment, artifact mapping, and collection and quantification.  Because we will require a space adequate to the task of producing and documenting stone tool production, Session 2 will take place in the Archaeology Lab in Building 110 (behind Module 86C, see Map).

Session 1: Introducing Stone Tools:

1.    Carefully review the "Flintknapping" video by Dr. Bruce Bradley and the "Ancient Cultures and Modern Chemistry" (ChemLab) video (or the Fall 1997 SBSC 224sl/324sl flintknapping demonstration video).  Be prepared to take detailed notes (for submission with your final portfolio) of the demonstration and the methods described.  Your description should be detailed enough to allow another person to use your notes to learn about stone tool production methods.

2.    After the video review and discussion, your professor will provide examples and a hands-on demonstration of the types of materials and tools used in the production of stone tools.  You should prepare notes based on your observations and your professor's demonstrations of the tools and techniques noted.  You should prepare responses to the following questions based on based on both the video and the demonstration:

    (a)    Briefly identify and define the following flintknapping methods: (1) hard-hammer percussion, (b) soft-hammer percussion, and (c) pressure flaking.

    (b)    Be prepared to discuss why each of the aforementioned methods were used.  In other words, when and why would an ancient stone tool producer use "hard-hammer percussion " versus "soft-hammer percussion" versus "pressure flaking"?

    (c)    How do archaeologists go about distinguishing tool types one from the other?  How do archaeologists go about identifying the function of stone tools from archaeological sites?

    (d)    How does "experimental archaeology" figure into the study of stone tools?  What value and insights are offered by experimental archaeology in so far as the interpretation and study of stone tools is concerned?

Session 2: Creating and Interpreting Stone Tools:

1.    For next week, please read and prepare notes based on Chapters 2 and 11 in the Whittaker book "Flintknapping."  While Chapter 2 will provide a basic introduction to the methods of stone tool production, Chapter 11 provides an overview of how archaeologists go about studying stone tools.  These notes will be required in your final portfolio.

2.    Meet at the usual class location, and be prepared to go to the Archaeology Lab in Building 110 about one half hour after the beginning of class.  Upon arrival at the usual class location, we will discuss the readings and take a roll call of all students present for the trip to the Archaeology Lab.  After the discussion, we will proceed to the Archaeology Lab.

3.    Because you will be handling sharp stone tools materials, you will be required to use gloves and goggles during the lab exercise.  In addition, you should come prepared with long pants so as to protect your knees from sharp obsidian glass fragments.

4.    During the lab exercise, you will first observe your professor's demonstration and take notes of any and all methods used in the demonstration.  You will be given an opportunity to experiment with some of the stone tool material if you choose to do so.

5.    At the end of the demonstration, you will be required to count, collect, and weigh the stone tool materials deposited in each 20 cm x 20 cm square indicated on the gridded tarp used for the demonstration.  As groups, you will submit your counts, weights, and notes on the demonstration via E-mail to: astv@archaeology.ml.org
